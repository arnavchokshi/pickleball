# Court + Net Calibration Research Findings — Deep-Read Synthesis

## 1. State of pickleball-specific prior art: essentially none

An extensive search (arXiv, GitHub, Roboflow Universe, blogs) as of July 2026 finds **no dedicated academic paper on pickleball court or net calibration**, analogous to what exists for tennis (PnLCalib/TennisCourtDetector) or soccer (PnLCalib/TVCalib/KpSFR). This absence was independently re-checked adversarially and confirmed: the closest artifacts are small Roboflow Universe keypoint datasets — e.g. "pickle-court-keypoints" (~1.1k images, CC BY 4.0, created ~March 2025) — and hobbyist YOLOv8/YOLOv11-pose projects with no reported calibration benchmarks (https://universe.roboflow.com/xuann-bacc-ujr91/pickle-court-keypoints-nluo7). A May 2026 Roboflow blog post describes an RF-DETR + Claude Sonnet 4.5 pipeline for pickleball *player-positioning analytics*, not court/net calibration (https://blog.roboflow.com/automate-pickleball-analytics/). **This means our roadmap is doing genuinely novel integration and modeling work, not porting an existing pickleball pipeline.**

## 2. Court keypoint/line detection: the points+lines paradigm is mature (soccer/tennis), unproven on handheld footage

The strongest, most benchmarked architecture family is the **PnLCalib / "No Bells, Just Whistles" lineage** (Gutiérrez-Pérez & Agudo). PnLCalib (arXiv:2404.08401, v5 dated 2026-03-10, published in *Computer Vision and Image Understanding* Vol. 267, Apr 2026) [VERIFIED, with corrections] uses a dual HRNetV2-W48 heatmap encoder-decoder — one head for a hierarchical grid of keypoints (line-line intersections, extended-line intersections, line-conic/ellipse intersections, plus auxiliary axis points), a second head for line-segment extremities — followed by an initial DLT+RANSAC pose solve and a novel non-linear PnL refinement that jointly minimizes a weighted point+line reprojection cost (α=0.6 line-weight, empirically tuned). On soccer benchmarks: single-view + PnL reaches Final Score (FS) 78.7–85.9 and JaC@5px 80.6–85.9% on SoccerNet SN22-test-center and WorldCup 2014 respectively, but **multi-view on the full, harder SN23-test set drops to FS 58.6–61.8** — a >20-point degradation once cameras move away from the clean center-camera framing (https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.08401). PnL refinement itself adds +5.7 to +8.3 FS points over keypoints-only baselines. Code is at https://github.com/mguti97/PnLCalib, **license GPL-2.0** (confirmed via direct LICENSE file fetch, despite the README being silent/contradictory on this point) — fine for internal-only use per our stated constraints, but relevant if code is ever shared externally. Inference runs **164–439 ms/frame (≈2.3–6 fps) on an RTX 2080 Ti**, not real-time, no built-in video/temporal amortization.

Critically for our floor comparison: **"A Universal Protocol to Benchmark Camera Calibration for Sports"** (arXiv:2404.09807, 2024) reports pixel-space SOTA soccer reprojection error of **1.44px best-case (pinhole+radial-distortion model, WC14/CARWC) up to 4.01–4.51px on the harder SoccerNet set** — this is 4–14x tighter than our current **19.8px p95**, but achieved on a much easier task: a huge planar field, fixed/PTZ elevated camera, and no close-range lens distortion. This gap is the central open question for any port: even best-in-class 2024–2026 sports calibration struggles to get much below 1.4px on an *easier* domain than ours.

Two lower-fidelity but relevant analogues: **TennisCourtDetector** (yastrebksv, 14-keypoint heatmap CNN, 8,841 broadcast images) achieves 0.936→0.963 precision and 2.83px→1.83px median distance with classical Hough-line-intersection postprocessing plus homography-based occlusion recovery — but **no license file exists** (GitHub API reports `license: null`), and critically, its "enhance net predictions" phrase refers to the *model's own predictions*, not net cord geometry — direct code inspection confirms there is **no net-height/post/sag logic anywhere in the repo** [VERIFIED by direct postprocess.py read]. A 2024 paper targeting exactly our harder "amateur/handheld" setting (arXiv:2404.06977) uses classical Hough-line detection plus pretrained shadow-removal (MTMT+ShadowFormer) and YOLOv5 net-detection, with a **color-based pixel filtration step** (flag as line-pixel if color mismatches the dominant court color but neighbors match): this raised accuracy on 6 of 7 amateur test courts (e.g. 0%→81.3%, 50.8%→100%) but **regressed on a two-tone Wimbledon-style court (96.0%→86.0%)** — a directly documented warning for our own tennis+pickleball overlaid-line scenario. No code was released for this paper.

## 3. Temporal stability across a handheld clip

Two papers directly address stabilizing calibration across video rather than solving each frame independently. **BroadTrack** (WACV 2025, arXiv:2412.01721, code https://github.com/evs-broadcast/BroadTrack) fuses an off-the-shelf field detector with a purpose-built camera+tripod motion model (8 params: focal length, k1, pan/tilt/roll, focal-point xyz), and **halves mean reprojection error versus prior SOTA (5.02px vs 10.28px NBJW) with a ~20-point JaC5 gain** on SoccerNet-gamestate [VERIFIED across two independent adversarial passes] — running at 16fps on HD footage, dual RTX 4090, unoptimized. It automatically reinitializes when field markings go sparse (33 times across 49 test sequences). **BHITK** (arXiv:2311.10361) wraps any per-frame homography detector in a two-stage Kalman filter modeling frame-to-frame homography as an affine transform plus explicit keypoint uncertainty — a general-purpose smoothing layer, though exact improvement numbers were not extractable from the abstract-only source reached. Neither has been tried with a device-native VIO pose stream (like ARKit) as the motion prior — that substitution appears to be open, unclaimed territory.

## 4. Gravity/pose priors: ARKit's advantage is real but unexploited in the literature

**PoseGravity** (arXiv:2405.12646, v3 dated 2026-06-30) gives a closed-form solver for absolute camera pose from points and/or lines given a *known* rotation axis — precisely our situation with ARKit-supplied gravity. Reference implementation is header-only C++ with Python bindings, **BSD-3-Clause** license (https://github.com/akschion/PoseGravity). No numeric accuracy/runtime benchmark table was retrievable from the sources reached in this pass, so its real-world gain over our current solver is architecturally plausible but empirically unverified. **GeoCalib** (ECCV 2024, arXiv:2409.06704) and its 2025 successor **AnyCalib** (arXiv:2503.12701, code Apache-2.0, github.com/javrtg/AnyCalib) jointly/independently estimate gravity + focal length + distortion from a single image; AnyCalib needs no gravity cue at all and, trained on only 54k synthetic images, matches or beats 3D foundation models trained on 3M+ images (0.69–1.79° angular error vs GeoCalib's 1.12–2.18° across LaMAR/MegaDepth/TartanAir/Stanford2D3D), at ~25ms/image on an RTX 4090. These tools solve **intrinsics only** — they are a plausible sanity-check/drift-corrector layer on top of sidecar ARKit intrinsics, not a court/net solution. Separately, basketball-court field-registration work (arXiv:2307.03353, 2023) independently notes that "mobile devices equipped with gravity sensors" can supply this constraint — external corroboration that our approach direction is recognized valid, though with no implementation detail. **No source found in this search (confirmed by adversarial re-check) fuses ARKit/ARCore gravity + intrinsics + plane-anchors with a court-line detector end-to-end for any sport** — this integration is novel work for us.

## 5. Net 3D geometry: the field's biggest open gap

Across every court-sport source surveyed (soccer, tennis, basketball, volleyball, table tennis), **net/net-cord 3D geometry (post height differential, sag curve, center strap) is unsolved**. TennisCourtDetector has zero net logic (verified directly). Volleyball net representations found — Szelag et al. 2019's fixed 12-camera rig (https://journals.pan.pl/Content/115253/PDF/opelre_2019_27_2_202-212.pdf), the asigatchov YOLOv11-pose repo, Roboflow Universe volleyball datasets — either omit the net entirely from calibration keypoints, or reduce it to **two flat pole-base points with no height/sag information**. The one cross-sport analogue worth borrowing from is **TT3D** (CVPRW 2025, arXiv:2504.10035, code at github.com/cogsys-tuebingen/tt3d, ball-detector withheld "under review"): a small 2M-param U-Net++/RegNetY table-segmentation net (0.92 mIoU, 70 FPS) feeds a PnPf solve reaching table-corner MAE of 2.39±1.47px with no manual calibration at inference — its **bounce-anchored** (not trajectory-start-anchored) 3D optimization trick is conceptually transferable to net-relative geometry problems. More directly relevant to net *sag*: **3D catenary curve fitting** (Madaan, Kaess, Scherer, ICRA 2019, https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~kaess/pub/Madaan19icra.pdf) reconstructs sagging wires from images with **known camera poses** using a parsimonious 5-parameter model (planar catenary + 3D rigid-frame transform) and a distance-transform-of-segmentation-mask reprojection loss that degrades gracefully with partial/occluded per-frame detections. Since ARKit already supplies known camera poses per frame, this recipe is directly adoptable *if* a net-cord segmentation model can first be built — itself an unsolved sub-problem (thin, low-contrast, often-occluded cord). No numeric accuracy table for the catenary method's own results was extractable in this pass (abstract-only access).

## 6. Court-profile library / re-identification

**PlanaReLoc** (CVPR 2026, arXiv:2603.20818) proposes 6-DoF relocalization via matching 3D planar primitives (not points) between a query frame and a stored planar map, in a learned embedding, **without per-scene retraining** — structurally matching our "re-identify a known court, reuse frozen calibration" goal, validated on ScanNet/12Scenes ("hundreds of scenes"). However, as of 2026-07-06 its repo (https://github.com/3dv-casia/PlanaReLoc) contains **only a README/docs — no code**, despite a promised release date of 2026-06-03 already passed. No numeric metrics were extractable. This is a "watch, don't block on" item.

## 7. Front-end line detectors (generic geometric primitives)

**ScaleLSD** (CVPR 2025, arXiv:2506.09369) is a self-supervised line-segment detector trained on 10M+ unlabeled real images, reported as the first deep LSD to outperform classical LSD across detection, single-view 3D geometry, and line-mapping tasks — a candidate generic front end upstream of a court-specific line-to-model assignment stage (we already hold this checkpoint locally). **DeepLSD** (CVPR 2023) and **GlueStick**/**LightGlueStick** (ICCV 2023 / Oct 2025, arXiv:2510.16438, 78.1 point AP / 74.6 line AP on ETH3D at ~47ms) round out the point+line matching toolkit, relevant if a re-identification or correspondence-matching stage (rather than per-frame heatmap regression) is chosen.

## 8. Bottom line for the accuracy-floor question

Our measured calibration reprojection p95 (~19.8px) roughly equals our ball-tracking F1@20px radius — meaning calibration error currently bounds both ball and player-placement accuracy. Every external benchmark surveyed operates on an *easier* domain (fixed/PTZ broadcast cameras, huge planar fields, clean single-color lines) than our handheld, lens-distorted, multi-color-overlaid, occlusion-prone pickleball setting, and even there, SOTA reprojection error only reaches 1.4–4.5px. This gap-in-domain-difficulty is the load-bearing uncertainty for any "port PnLCalib" decision: there is no existing evidence that a retrained points+lines network would clear our 19.8px bar by a wide enough margin to justify the retraining cost, absent a pilot experiment.


## Ranked options

### Port PnLCalib's dual-heatmap (keypoints+lines) + PnL non-linear refinement architecture, retrained on a synthetic pickleball generator + limited real captures
- **what:** Retrain HRNetV2-W48-style keypoint-heatmap and line-extremity-heatmap heads on a pickleball-specific hierarchical keypoint grid (all corners, baselines, sidelines, kitchen lines, centerline, service lines derived via line-line/line-conic intersections), then run the same points+lines non-linear refinement (alpha-weighted point+line reprojection cost) in place of (or alongside) our current geometric solver / court_unet_v2.
- **evidence:** PnLCalib SV+PnL reaches FS=85.9/JaC5=85.9% on WC14 and FS=78.7-79.5 on SN22-test-center [VERIFIED numbers, adversarially corrected for exact figures], with the PnL refinement module alone adding +5.7 to +8.3 FS points over keypoints-only. https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.08401 ; code GPL-2.0 at https://github.com/mguti97/PnLCalib
- **expected_gain:** Potentially large: this is the most mature, best-benchmarked points+lines architecture in the literature and the only one with a directly comparable 'JaCgamma at pixel thresholds' framework we can adopt to score ourselves against a recognized standard; the joint line+point refinement is architecturally suited to our full-court-line taxonomy (not just 4-15 sparse points).
- **cost:** High: requires a full retrain (new keypoint/line taxonomy, new synthetic+real training data, GPU time on A100, likely multiple iteration cycles) since no pretrained pickleball weights exist anywhere; runtime is 164-439ms/frame on an RTX2080Ti-class GPU, non-trivial at our clip volumes.
- **risk:** Even SOTA soccer calibration (a much easier planar/fixed-camera domain) only reaches 1.4-4.5px reprojection error at best (arXiv:2404.09807); handheld pickleball with lens distortion, occlusion, and multi-color overlaid lines could land well above our current 19.8px p95, i.e. a full retrain might not clearly beat what we have without first validating on a pilot dataset. Also GPL-2.0 licensing carries no internal-use legal risk here per stated project constraints (internal-only acceptable) but constrains any future external distribution.
- **confidence:** med

### Fuse ARKit gravity as a hard axis-prior into pose estimation via a PoseGravity-style closed-form points+lines solver
- **what:** Replace or augment the current geometric solver's rotation-estimation step with a closed-form solver that takes ARKit gravity as a known-axis constraint plus detected court points/lines, reducing the DOF that must be estimated from image evidence alone.
- **evidence:** PoseGravity (arXiv:2405.12646) gives closed-form point+line pose-with-known-axis solutions (hyperbola/unit-circle intersection), reference implementation is header-only C++ with Python bindings, BSD-3-Clause, claimed 'orders of magnitude faster' than general PnP/PnL solvers per README (unverified numerically in this pass). Basketball-court literature (arXiv:2307.03353) independently corroborates that mobile gravity sensors are a recognized, valid constraint for field-registration pose recovery.
- **expected_gain:** Could reduce reprojection error specifically in the axis/tilt dimension where lens distortion and handheld shake hurt most, since gravity removes one full rotational DOF from the search space per frame; low integration cost since ARKit gravity is already captured by our sidecar.
- **cost:** Low-medium: integrating a header-only C++ solver (with Python bindings) is a modest engineering lift; no retraining needed since it's a geometric (not learned) solver.
- **risk:** No numeric accuracy/runtime benchmark table for PoseGravity was retrievable in this research pass -- the expected-gain claim is architecturally plausible but empirically unverified; would need our own pilot test to confirm it beats the current solver.
- **confidence:** med

### Adopt BroadTrack-style temporal fusion: replace independent per-frame calibration with a physically-constrained motion-model + Kalman/optimization fusion using ARKit's continuous 6-DOF pose stream in place of BroadTrack's tripod prior
- **what:** Instead of (or in addition to) per-frame court detection, model frame-to-frame calibration drift using ARKit's VIO pose stream as the motion prior (replacing BroadTrack's fixed-tripod assumption), re-anchoring to absolute court coordinates whenever per-frame detector confidence is high, with automatic reinitialization on low-confidence frames.
- **evidence:** BroadTrack (WACV2025, arXiv:2412.01721) halves mean reprojection error (5.02px vs 10.28px NBJW) and gains ~20pts JaC5 on SoccerNet-gamestate purely by fusing a strong physical camera-motion prior with an off-the-shelf field detector [VERIFIED by 2 adversarial passes]; BHITK (arXiv:2311.10361) independently validates the general Kalman-filtering-of-homography-across-frames approach for reducing jitter/error over per-frame-independent solves.
- **expected_gain:** High if realized: we already have a MUCH stronger motion prior than BroadTrack's fixed-tripod assumption (full continuous ARKit VIO pose, not just a rigid mount model), so the ceiling for improvement could exceed BroadTrack's soccer results; this directly targets our stated pain point of handheld camera motion.
- **cost:** Medium-high: requires building the fusion/optimization layer (not just borrowing a checkpoint) since no published work does exactly this ARKit+court-detector fusion; needs careful re-anchoring/reinit logic design and validation across our clip library.
- **risk:** No existing implementation to reference for the exact ARKit-pose-as-motion-prior substitution -- this is novel integration work, so engineering time could be underestimated; also depends on having a working per-frame court/line detector to fuse with in the first place (a precondition, not a replacement).
- **confidence:** med

### Use AnyCalib/GeoCalib as an intrinsics sanity-check/drift-corrector layered on top of sidecar ARKit intrinsics, rather than as the primary calibration method
- **what:** Run AnyCalib (or GeoCalib) per-frame or per-segment as an independent single-image intrinsics estimate, cross-checking against sidecar-reported ARKit intrinsics especially near frame edges where lens distortion is worst and our reprojection error is highest.
- **evidence:** AnyCalib (arXiv:2503.12701, code Apache-2.0 at github.com/javrtg/AnyCalib) achieves 0.69-1.79 deg angular error across LaMAR/MegaDepth/TartanAir/Stanford2D3D with only 54k training images, beating 3D foundation models trained on 3M+ images, at ~25ms/image on an RTX4090; requires no gravity cue (unlike GeoCalib) and supports 14+ camera/distortion models without retraining.
- **expected_gain:** Low-medium: this only addresses intrinsics (focal length, distortion), not court/net structure -- won't directly move our 19.8px p95 court-point reprojection floor, but could catch sidecar intrinsics errors or drift that compound into that floor, particularly at frame edges.
- **cost:** Low: ~25ms/frame inference cost, permissive Apache-2.0 license, straightforward drop-in given a released model + weights.
- **risk:** Marginal expected impact on the actual court/net calibration problem since it doesn't touch keypoint/line detection or net geometry at all; could be a distraction from higher-leverage options if resources are scarce.
- **confidence:** low

### Adapt the catenary-curve-fitting recipe (Madaan et al., ICRA 2019) for net top-cord sag reconstruction, using ARKit poses + a to-be-built net-cord segmentation mask
- **what:** Fit a parsimonious multi-parameter catenary-like model (extending the 5-parameter wire model to account for the net's asymmetric loading: fixed 36in post heights, 34in center sag, center-strap pull) to a per-frame net-cord segmentation mask, using the distance-transform reprojection-error technique across frames with known (ARKit) camera poses.
- **evidence:** Madaan/Kaess/Scherer ICRA 2019 (https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~kaess/pub/Madaan19icra.pdf) demonstrates real-time multi-view wire/catenary reconstruction from known-pose images using only 5 parameters and a distance-transform loss that gracefully tolerates partial/occluded detections -- directly structurally analogous to our net-sag problem, and no court-sport literature surveyed (soccer, tennis, basketball, volleyball, table tennis) has solved 3D net-height/sag geometry at all.
- **expected_gain:** Potentially high and differentiated: this would be the FIRST solved 3D net-geometry model in the court-sport CV literature per our survey (volleyball/tennis net representations found are at most 2 flat pole points, no height/sag) -- closing our stated net_plane-only gap into full 3D net geometry (posts, cord height differential, sag curve, center strap).
- **cost:** Medium: requires building a net-cord pixel/line segmentation model first (unsolved elsewhere, so this is new training-data + model work), then adapting the 5-parameter catenary fit; ARKit poses are already available so the known-pose precondition is satisfied.
- **risk:** The segmentation-mask prerequisite is itself an unsolved sub-problem (thin, often occluded, low-contrast cord against variable backgrounds) -- risk of underestimating how hard reliable net-cord segmentation is before the catenary fit can even be attempted; no numeric accuracy results for the catenary method itself were extractable from the sources reached (abstract-only for the ICRA paper's quantitative section).
- **confidence:** med

### Build a color-based line-family pre-filter (à la the amateur-tennis paper) as a cheap first-pass before any deep line-family classifier, tuned to our specific pickleball/tennis color conventions
- **what:** Implement a per-pixel dominant-court-color detector + neighborhood-based line-pixel flagging (pixel is a line pixel if it does NOT match dominant court color but IS surrounded by pixels that do) as an inexpensive preprocessing stage to separate pickleball lines from overlaid tennis/badminton lines of a different color.
- **evidence:** arXiv:2404.06977 (2024) raised per-court accuracy on 6/7 amateur courts (e.g. 0.0%->81.3%, 50.8%->100%) using exactly this technique, but REGRESSED on a two-tone Wimbledon-style court (96.0%->86.0%) -- a direct, documented failure mode analogous to our multi-purpose-court concern.
- **expected_gain:** Low-medium and narrow: cheap to implement and could help on genuinely single-color-dominant courts, but the one documented multi-color test case in the literature shows this approach specifically breaks down on the two-tone scenario most similar to ours.
- **cost:** Very low: classical CV, no training, fast to prototype and test against our own footage.
- **risk:** Documented regression on multi-color courts directly undermines applicability to our stated core problem (pickleball-on-tennis-court, contrasting colors); no code released for this paper, so we'd be re-implementing from a paper description with no numeric validation of our own reimplementation.
- **confidence:** low

### Wait/monitor for PlanaReLoc code release before building the court-profile-library (re-identify known court, reuse frozen calibration) feature
- **what:** Defer building a from-scratch court re-identification/plane-matching system and instead track PlanaReLoc's promised code release, given its plane-primitive (not point) matching against a stored 3D planar map requires no per-scene retraining -- structurally matching our exact 'known courts' reuse goal.
- **evidence:** PlanaReLoc (CVPR 2026 accepted, arXiv:2603.20818) claims plane-primitive matching in a learned unified embedding space works without per-scene training, validated on ScanNet/12Scenes ('hundreds of scenes'), but as of 2026-07-06 the GitHub repo (https://github.com/3dv-casia/PlanaReLoc) contains only a README/docs -- no code, despite a passed promised release date of 2026-06-03.
- **expected_gain:** Potentially high if the promised generalization claims hold (no per-scene retraining is exactly what a growing multi-court user base needs) -- but currently zero, since nothing is usable yet.
- **cost:** Low cost to monitor (a scheduled check), but high opportunity cost if we block the court-profile-library roadmap item on an unreleased artifact with a track record of a missed deadline.
- **risk:** Unknown release timeline, unknown final code quality/license, and unvalidated real-world numbers (no metrics were extractable from the sources reached) -- betting the roadmap on this specific artifact is risky; a simpler in-house approach (e.g. reference-frame feature matching, or persisting ARKit world anchors across sessions) might ship faster with lower risk.
- **confidence:** low

Volleyball net keypoint schemas found (Szelag et al. 2019 fixed-12-camera rig; asigatchov YOLOv11-pose repo; Roboflow Universe volleyball datasets) either omit the net entirely or reduce it to 2 flat pole-base points -- none encode height, center-vs-post differential, or cord sag.
- TT3D (CVPRW 2025, arXiv:2504.10035, code https://github.com/cogsys-tuebingen/tt3d, ball-detector component withheld 'under review') is the closest cross-sport analogue for our net-sag problem: a 2M-param U-Net++/RegNetY table-segmentation net (0.92 mIoU, 70 FPS) feeds a PnPf solve (alternating PnP + focal-length refinement) achieving table-corner MAE of 2.39+/-1.47px with NO manual calibration input at inference. Its bounce-anchored (not trajectory-start-anchored) 3D reconstruction trick is a transferable idea.
- 3D catenary-curve fitting (ICRA 2019, Madaan/Kaess/Scherer, https://www.cs.cmu.edu/~kaess/pub/Madaan19icra.pdf) reconstructs sagging wires from images with KNOWN camera poses using a 5-parameter model (planar catenary + 3D rigid-frame transform) and a distance-transform-of-segmentation-mask reprojection loss that tolerates partial/occluded per-frame detections -- structurally the closest published recipe for fitting our net's sag curve (36in post height / 34in center + center-strap pull), IF we already have camera poses (which ARKit supplies) and a per-frame net-cord segmentation mask.
- The amateur/handheld-camera tennis line-detection paper (arXiv:2404.06977, 2024) is the one paper explicitly targeting our 'harder than broadcast' setting; its pixel-color-filtration method (flag pixel as line if it does NOT match the dominant court color and is surrounded by neighbors that DO) raised per-court accuracy from 0-96% to 81-100% on 6 of 7 amateur courts, but REGRESSED on a two-tone (Wimbledon-style) court (96.0% -> 86.0%) -- a direct warning sign for our multi-purpose tennis+pickleball overlay scenario. No code released, no formal accuracy metric beyond a custom 'score-overlap' heuristic.
- 'A Universal Protocol to Benchmark Camera Calibration for Sports' (arXiv:2404.09807, 2024) argues existing IoU/homography-only benchmarks unfairly favor planar methods and proposes JaCgamma-family metrics explicitly designed to also capture non-planar structures (like our net) and lens distortion -- directly relevant methodology reference if we want a rigorous internal benchmark protocol.
- PlanaReLoc (CVPR 2026, arXiv:2603.20818) proposes plane-primitive (not point) matching for 6-DoF relocalization against a stored 3D planar map, with NO per-scene retraining required -- structurally matches our planned 'court-profile library: re-identify a known court, reuse frozen calibration' feature, but as of 2026-07-06 its code is NOT yet released (promised 3-Jun-2026 date has passed with only a README/docs in the repo). https://github.com/3dv-casia/PlanaReLoc
- ScaleLSD (CVPR 2025, arXiv:2506.09369) is a self-supervised line-segment detector trained on 10M+ unlabeled real images, reported as the first deep LSD to beat classical LSD across detection + single-view 3D geometry + line matching/mapping tasks -- a candidate generic line front-end upstream of a court-specific line-to-model assignment stage (we already have this checkpoint locally per project notes).
- LightGlueStick (arXiv:2510.16438, Oct 2025) is a faster successor to GlueStick for joint point+line matching: 78.1 point AP / 74.6 line AP on ETH3D at ~47ms average -- relevant if we need point+line correspondence matching (e.g. for re-identifying a known court's line pattern) rather than per-frame heatmap regression.
- Basketball-court field-registration literature (arXiv:2307.03353, 2023) explicitly notes that mobile devices with gravity sensors can supply the vertical-axis constraint needed for pose recovery from field registration -- direct corroboration (from an unrelated sport) that our ARKit-gravity approach is a recognized, valid calibration lever, though no implementation details are given.
- Our own measured accuracy floor (calibration reprojection p95 ~19.8px) roughly equals our ball F1@20px radius, meaning calibration error currently bounds BOTH ball tracking and player-placement accuracy -- this is the single most decision-relevant internal number to compare against every external benchmark above.

## Open questions

- Would porting PnLCalib's HRNetV2-W48 dual-heatmap (keypoints+lines) + PnL non-linear refinement architecture to a pickleball keypoint/line taxonomy (retrained from scratch on synthetic + limited real pickleball data) plausibly beat our current court_unet_v2/geometric-solver's 19.8px p95, given PnLCalib's own soccer numbers (1.4-4.5px SOTA floor on an EASIER planar/fixed-camera task) suggest real risk that handheld pickleball footage could land well above that -- is a pilot retrain worth the GPU hours before committing further roadmap time?
- Is BroadTrack's tripod-motion-model idea (fusing a physical mounting/motion prior with per-frame detections via Kalman/optimization) portable to our handheld setting by substituting the tripod prior with ARKit's continuous 6-DOF VIO pose stream -- and would that materially reduce jitter/error versus per-frame-independent calibration, given no published work has tried this exact substitution?
- Should we prioritize building a from-scratch pickleball-specific keypoint+line dataset (synthetic generator + owner-captured real clips) modeled on PnLCalib's hierarchical keypoint-grid methodology (line-line, extended-line, and line-conic intersections) now, given zero existing pickleball academic datasets/checkpoints exist to fine-tune from?
- For net 3D geometry (posts, top-cord sag, center strap), is the catenary-curve-fit-with-known-camera-poses recipe (Madaan et al., ICRA 2019) directly adoptable given we already have ARKit poses per frame, or does it require first building a reliable per-frame net-cord segmentation mask (unsolved for any court sport in the literature surveyed) as a prerequisite -- and is that segmentation task easier or harder than the general court-line segmentation problem?
- Does PoseGravity's closed-form points+lines-with-known-axis solver, when driven by ARKit gravity, give a measurable accuracy or speed improvement over our current geometric solver, given no numeric benchmark table for PoseGravity could be retrieved in this research pass -- would a direct empirical test be needed before committing?
- How much would the amateur-tennis paper's regression on two-tone/multi-color courts (96.0%->86.0%) generalize to our exact scenario (pickleball lines overlaid on a tennis court in a contrasting but different color scheme, e.g. Seattle's white-tennis/light-green-pickleball convention) -- is a learned per-pixel line-family classifier (segmentation-based, per the Jouini/Elloumi/Chaieb 2024 framework) necessary, or could a simpler color-based heuristic filter suffice given our specific court color conventions?
- Is it worth polling/waiting on PlanaReLoc's code release (promised 3-Jun-2026, still unreleased as of 5-Jul-2026) for the court-profile-library re-identification feature, or should we proceed now with a simpler approach (e.g. feature-matching against a stored reference frame, or ARKit world-anchor persistence) given uncertain timeline?
- What is the actual runtime/accuracy tradeoff of running a PnLCalib-style dual-heatmap network on-device vs. cloud A100, and would batching across our ~6-8 min/clip processing pipeline (per current speed-lane work) absorb the extra 164-439ms/frame cost, or does it become a bottleneck given we already have tight per-clip runtime budgets from other lanes?
- Has anyone validated single-handheld-phone LiDAR (not multi-camera arena rigs like BasketLiDAR) for direct court-plane/net-plane metric fitting -- this search found no such source, and our app has LiDAR on Pro iPhones already; is a targeted internal experiment warranted before further literature search?
