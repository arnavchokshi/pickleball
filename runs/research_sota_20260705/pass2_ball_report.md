## Pass-2 synthesis: ball tracking/3D trajectory/prediction for racket sports

Everything below is new relative to pass 1 (which already covered WASB-SBDT, TrackNetV2-V5, BlurBall, TOTNet, RacketVision-as-name-only, MonoTrack, TT3D, TT4D, UpliftingTableTennis, Where Is The Ball, SST, Vandeghen, RF-DETR, SAM3, TrackNet-Pickleball, SAHI at a shallow level — this pass adds depth, numbers, adjacent papers, and adversarial corrections). Organized by pipeline sub-problem.

### 1. 2D ball detection/tracking robustness (occlusion, blur, camera motion)

Pass 1 knew WASB-SBDT/TrackNet-family/BlurBall exist; this pass adds the specific mechanisms and honest caveats. WASB-SBDT (Tarashima et al., BMVC 2023, arXiv:2311.05237, github.com/nttcom/WASB-SBDT, MIT) reports +7.8 to +16.8% AP over 6 prior baselines (DeepBall, DeepBall-Large, BallSeg, TrackNetV2, ResTrackNetV2, MonoTrack) across 5 sports at τ=4px, via three specific levers: stride-removal for high-resolution features, real-valued (Gaussian) heatmap ground truth with quality-focal loss plus Hard-to-Localize Sample Mining, and a constant-acceleration (not Kalman) online motion filter at inference — worth checking whether our current integration actually uses the online-filter/HLSM levers rather than just the vanilla weights. Exact per-dataset F1/AP numbers were not extractable this pass (PDF/README render without the results table) — flag as still unverified beyond the abstract claim.

A dedicated occlusion paper, arXiv:2508.09650, introduces visibility-weighted loss + 3D-conv temporal modeling + a new occlusion-rich Table Tennis Australia/Paralympic dataset (9,159 samples, 1,996 occlusion cases) with cross-sport (tennis/badminton/table-tennis) generalization evaluation — directly on our "ball vanishes behind paddle/player" failure mode, though exact metrics weren't extracted in the source data provided.

Camera-motion robustness (our actual failure mode, since we shoot handheld iPhone, not static broadcast) is addressed by "One-Shot Badminton Shuttle Detection for Mobile Robots" (arXiv:2603.06691, Mar 2026): fine-tuned YOLOv8 on a new 20,510-frame egocentric/moving-camera dataset across 11 backgrounds, reporting F1=0.86 in-distribution vs F1=0.70 on fully unseen backgrounds — a useful external benchmark for how much our zero-shot wall (per pickleball-ball-chain-state memory) should be expected to close with in-domain fine-tuning.

TrackNetV5-SDK (arXiv:2512.02789, Dec 2025) adds Motion Direction Decoupling (signed polarity fields replacing V4's unsigned frame-differencing) and a Residual-Driven Spatio-Temporal Refinement transformer head for occlusion recovery, reporting F1=0.9859/Acc=0.9733 on the TrackNetV2 tennis dataset with only +3.7% FLOPs over V4. Caveat: the maintained "SDK" repo (Shanghai Code Zero Sports Technology) is "academic/technical-exchange only, all rights reserved" — usable for our private use but not for redistribution, and requires CUDA 12.6/Torch 2.9 (verify against our A100 environment).

Point-tracking foundation models are an adjacent lens: DeepMind's TAPNext++ (arXiv:2604.10582, Apr 2026) extends effective tracking from ~150 to 4000+ frames and adds an explicit "Re-Detection Average Jaccard" metric for points that disappear and reappear — exactly the ball-vanishes-and-returns pattern within a rally — at 191 FPS for 1024 points, but no code/weights confirmed yet. Two FMOX benchmark papers (arXiv:2512.09633, arXiv:2509.06536) empirically show which SAM2-family trackers (DAM4SAM, SAMURAI, EfficientTAM) survive fast-moving/blur-heavy objects, giving a template for stress-testing our own ensemble.

### 2. 2D→3D ball trajectory lift (the core bottleneck)

TT3D (arXiv:2504.10035, code at github.com/cogsys-tuebingen/tt3d) and its successor TT4D (arXiv:2605.01234, ~May 2026, no code yet) are the deepest new material here. TT3D calibrates the camera purely from detected table/court geometry (U-Net++ segmentation, 0.92 mIoU, 70fps; corner MAE 2.39±1.47px; ~3° rotation error) then solves for the bounce state minimizing physics-model reprojection error, achieving 8.9cm (side view, clean) to 29.8cm (back view, noisy) 3D trajectory MAE on synthetic-derived benchmarks. TT4D inverts the pipeline order — lifting the *entire* unsegmented 2D ball track to 3D first via a transformer with Disentangled Context Embedding + Deferred Upsampling Token Attention + RoPE-on-timestamp (not frame-index, handling variable FPS) before segmenting shots — and reports materially better real-world numbers: 2.35±1.03cm 3D position error (no augmentation), degrading gracefully to 3.78±2.15cm under combined half-FPS + 10% missing-detection stress, and explicitly beats TT3D ~2x head-to-head (15.09cm vs 29.86cm, side-view). It also validates racket-impact orientation/velocity (26.4°±4.4°, 0.58±0.40 m/s vs 92 mocap strokes) purely from reconstructed ball physics with no racket vision — a useful sanity-check methodology for our own 6-DOF paddle work, though its own accuracy there is coarser than our current fused estimator (~5°/frame jitter).

"Where Is The Ball" (arXiv:2506.05763, VISTEC, project page where-is-the-ball.github.io, code "coming soon") introduces a camera-location/orientation/focal-length-independent plane-point 4D representation — the physical parabola projects to a 2D parabola on a vertical reference plane regardless of camera pose — trained purely on synthetic Unity data, and beats a physics-only baseline (SynthNet) 5.7x on real-tennis landing error (0.63m vs 3.58m, T.acc 87.21%). Component ablations show the Height Network (depth/height estimation) is the single dominant term: removing it inflates NRMSE from 0.05 to 6.26 on mocap data — i.e., monocular depth/height disambiguation, not trajectory smoothing, is where most of the error lives. No code/weights are public yet, so this is a spike/reimplementation candidate, not a drop-in.

The synthetic-training recipe generalizes across several 2026 papers: Uplifting Table Tennis (arXiv:2511.20250, WACV 2026, project page kiedani.github.io/WACV2026, code linked) trains its 2D→3D+spin transformer exclusively on 140k MuJoCo-simulated trajectories, explicitly engineered for robustness to missing detections and variable frame rate (spin-classification F1 collapses from 1.000 to 0.598 under corruption without this design) — reporting 12.28±10.84px reprojection error / 89.5% spin accuracy (TTHQ) and 9.41±16.90px / 97.1% (TTST) full-pipeline. A related MuJoCo-based synthetic generator (arXiv:2504.19863) produces 50,000 physically valid trajectories reusing DeepMind robot-table-tennis sim parameters, hitting 92.0% real-data spin accuracy and 5.5cm 3D error via synthetic-to-real transfer with targeted augmentation (motion blur, sudden termination) — none of these use real 3D ground truth, directly matching our scarce-data constraint, but all require re-deriving pickleball-specific ball physics (mass, restitution, drag/lift — see §4) before reuse.

A differentiable-rendering alternative worth flagging: PMGS (arXiv:2508.02660) and its successor PEGS (arXiv:2511.17116) reconstruct fast rigid-body projectile motion via 3D Gaussian Splatting with an explicit Newtonian acceleration-consistency loss term plus Kalman fusion of multiple noisy pose observations — architecturally the closest thing to "differentiable version of our physics arc solver + multi-detector fusion," but neither has released code, and it's unconfirmed whether either is monocular.

### 3. Trajectory/shot forecasting (the "PREDICTION" half of our stated domain — largely new to pass 2)

RacketVision (arXiv:2511.17045, AAAI 2026 oral, code+data at github.com/OrcustD/RacketVision, HF: linfeng302/RacketVision) is a 1,672-clip, 3-sport (table tennis/tennis/badminton) benchmark unifying ball tracking, 5-keypoint racket pose, and future-trajectory forecasting, with released checkpoints for all three tasks. Its CrossLSTM-Attn baseline shows a concrete, reusable design lesson: naively concatenating ball-history and racket-pose features actively *hurts* trajectory forecasting versus a ball-only baseline (e.g. badminton short-horizon ADE 37.5→45.7 with naive concat), while cross-attention fusion (racket pose as Key/Value, ball trajectory as Query) recovers and modestly beats ball-only (37.0). Full ADE/FDE numbers: short-horizon (Hist=20/Fut=5) 38.3/60.4 (table tennis), 22.8/35.7 (tennis), 37.0/59.3 (badminton); long-horizon (Hist=80/Fut=20) 101.3/161.3, 55.5/94.7, 114.6/187.6 respectively.

LATTE-MV (arXiv:2503.20936, CVPR 2025, code at github.com/sastry-group/LATTE-MV) is the closest match-scale demonstration of our full domain statement: it reconstructs 3D ball+player trajectories from ~50 hours of ordinary monocular table-tennis video (73,222 exchanges, no special rig) and trains an uncertainty-aware anticipatory controller that raises simulated return rate against fast hits from 49.9% to 59.0%. A very recent adjacent idea, SPAD (arXiv:2606.11464, Jun 2026), trains a transformer purely on real ball-flight data to close the sim-to-real gap of a physics simulator by "swapping" it in at deployment time — directly relevant if our physics arc solver ever needs hardening against real-world deviations from its assumed model.

### 4. Ball physics / aerodynamics — pickleball-specific numbers now exist [VERIFIED with corrections]

Two publications give pickleball-specific (not borrowed) drag/lift coefficients. Steyn et al. (arXiv:2501.00163, Dec 2024/Jan 2025) measured Cd=0.30±0.02 and Cl=0.195·S from 6 outdoor-ball free-flight trajectories, explicitly claiming to be the first Cd/Cl determination for a pickleball. Lindsey (Tennis Warehouse University, 12 May 2025, twu.tennis-warehouse.com/learning_center/pickleball/pickleball_aerodynamics.php) independently ran a larger study (86 trajectories, two 300fps cameras, RK4 fit, ~15mm mean endpoint error): Cd≈0.45 (SD 0.092) indoor 26-hole balls vs Cd≈0.33 (SD 0.083) outdoor 40-hole balls, roughly constant across Reynolds numbers; lift shows topspin/backspin asymmetry (topspin scales linearly with spin number S, backspin flatter near Cl≈0.2) with nonzero lift near zero spin, which the author attributes to a gravity-induced airflow-asymmetry mechanism rather than classical symmetric Magnus theory. Adversarial verification confirmed the numeric claims verbatim against the primary source but flagged two corrections worth carrying forward: (a) Lindsey's study is NOT the first pickleball-specific parameterization (Steyn's is, and is cited by Lindsey); outdoor Cd values cross-corroborate (0.33 vs 0.30±0.02) but (b) the indoor Cd and the lift-asymmetry/"Magnus-contradiction" mechanism are unreplicated, single-source, non-peer-reviewed claims (TWU is a web learning-center article, author acknowledges ChatGPT-4 editing assistance) — treat drag magnitudes as reasonably solid, treat the lift-asymmetry mechanism as a hypothesis pending independent confirmation before hard-coding it into the arc solver's lift model.

### 5. Court/scene calibration (new depth beyond pass-1's court-autofind knowledge)

TT3D's table-corner-detection recipe (U-Net++ segmentation → contour/Hough → PnPf) is the most directly portable court-calibration technique found, real-time (70fps) with code released. Two badminton/tennis-specific court-registration papers were also surfaced: CourtKeyNet (Machine Learning with Applications, 2026, DOI 10.1016/j.mlwa.2026.100884) claims ~3.2px vs YOLOv8s-pose's 5.6px average keypoint error, but adversarial verification found the exact numeric claims (43% reduction, KLA@0.05 +8.5%, CD-IoU +12.3%) **not independently verifiable** — the paper itself is real and paywalled, but the specific figures came only from an AI web-search summarizer with no accessible primary table; the architecture claims (Octave Feature Extractor, Polar Transform Attention, Quadrilateral Constraint Module) are corroborated via the authors' own GitHub/HuggingFace, but treat the accuracy numbers as unverified. Jouini et al. (AIAI 2024, Springer, DOI 10.1007/978-3-031-63219-8_2) combines a segmentation encoder-decoder with homography estimation for badminton/tennis court registration on a 564-image dataset — claim is [VERIFIED] via HAL open-access metadata for authorship/architecture/dataset, but exact accuracy numbers remain inaccessible (Springer paywall, OpenReview bot-wall).

### 6. Data scarcity mitigation (directly on our stated constraint)

Vandeghen et al.'s confidence-weighted teacher-student semi-supervised framework (arXiv:2204.06859, code github.com/rvandeghen/SST, BSD-3) is the most transferable technique for our "owner in-domain data" bottleneck: three loss parametrizations let a student network discount low-confidence teacher pseudo-labels rather than trusting them uniformly, established as the first SoccerNet-v3 detection benchmark at 52.3% mAP (soccer-domain number, not a transferable target). New (mostly small/noisy) datasets worth noting: PadelTracker100 (Zenodo/PMC, Feb 2026, CC BY 4.0, ~100k frames, ball+17-keypoint pose+homography+shot labels from two full padel matches) is the closest racket-sport analog to pickleball court geometry; Roboflow Universe now has several genuinely pickleball-specific ball/paddle/player detection sets (263-630 images, non-benchmarked) — useful as supplementary negatives, not a replacement for owner captures.

### 7. Detection backbone

RF-DETR (github.com/roboflow/rf-detr, ICLR 2026, Apache-2.0) adds a keypoint head (RF-DETR Keypoint Preview, 71.8 AP50:95 on COCO person keypoints at 9.7ms/T4, beating YOLO26x-pose) alongside its detection/segmentation heads, all sharing a DINOv2 backbone — RF100-VL cross-domain results (57.1-60.6 mAP50:95 across 100 real-world datasets) make the N/S/M sizes a strong fine-tuning candidate for paddle/court-keypoint detection given our scarce labeled data, though the keypoint head lacks hand/finger keypoints so cannot replace SAM-3D-Body for the paddle's finger-pose signal.
