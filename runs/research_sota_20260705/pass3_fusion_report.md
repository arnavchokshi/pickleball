2026-07-06. 29 agents.


# Fusion / Global-Optimization Stage: Findings Report

## 1. The core architectural pattern that matches our goal: contact-coupled joint optimization

The single closest published analog to our "combine-everything" pillar is **JOSH** (Joint Optimization for 4D Human-Scene Reconstruction in the Wild, ICLR 2026, arXiv 2501.02158). JOSH takes off-the-shelf per-subsystem outputs — dense scene point clouds (MASt3R/DUSt3R), human mesh recovery (VIMO/TRAM), and contact predictions (DECO, BSTRO) — purely as *initialization*, then runs one joint optimization stage over scene geometry, camera trajectory, and human motion simultaneously, using human-scene **contact** as the coupling residual rather than compositing independently-solved subsystems (https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.02158, https://arxiv.org/html/2501.02158). This is architecturally exactly what our stage-sequential pipeline (court→camera→human→ball→paddle, composited only at the end) is missing.

On SLOPER4D, an ablation isolates the value of contact constraints: a no-contact-loss baseline gets WA-MPJPE100=314.3mm; adding a contact-**scale** hinge loss alone drops this to 148.9mm (53% relative improvement); the full joint optimization further reduces it to 120.0mm [VERIFIED core comparison — adversarial check confirms these numbers are correct but are SLOPER4D-specific, not aggregated across SLOPER4D/EMDB-2/RICH as an earlier draft of this finding implied]. On the same dataset, contact-aware joint optimization (JOSH3, initialized from VIMO+MASt3R) reduces foot-floating-rate from 9.0% to 2.9% and cuts scene Chamfer distance error 70.1% and WA-MPJPE 46.6% versus the non-joint SynCHMR★ baseline — again a SLOPER4D-only figure, not a cross-dataset average (https://arxiv.org/html/2501.02158). On EMDB (25 sequences), JOSH's WA-MPJPE100=68.9mm beats prior SOTA TRAM's 76.4mm and WHAM's ~131mm; on RICH, JOSH's WA-MPJPE100=89.0mm beats SLAHMR's 132.2mm.

JOSH's own stated limitation is directly relevant to our failure modes: "*JOSH works best when both the contact points on the human and the scene are visible in the 2D image*" — i.e. performance depends on visible contact, exactly the condition that fails during fast net-side ball occlusion or paddle-grip ambiguity in our footage. JOSH also documents chunking sequences ≥200 frames sequentially rather than doing full global bundle adjustment — the same long-clip problem our speed lane has already hit. Code is public (github.com/genforce/JOSH, 217 stars) but the GitHub license API returns `null` — no LICENSE file — so legal status is unconfirmed despite public code. Inference: JOSH's full optimizer runs at 0.8 FPS on an RTX4090 (offline-appropriate for our A100 budget); its distilled feed-forward student JOSH3R runs at 15.4 FPS at real accuracy cost (SLOPER4D WA-MPJPE100 rises to 271–438mm depending on the report) (https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.02158, https://vail-ucla.github.io/JOSH).

A newer sibling, **UniCon3R** (arXiv 2604.19923, April 2026, authors include Michael J. Black), names our exact failure modes verbatim ("bodies float above the ground or penetrate parts of the scene") and proposes inferring 4D contact jointly with pose in a single feed-forward pass rather than post-hoc IK cleanup — but as of this check its code/weights are not yet released and no numeric metrics were retrievable from the PDF (which exceeded fetch size limits), so this is a design-pattern reference only, not yet actionable (https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.19923).

## 2. Is joint optimization still winning over feed-forward alternatives?

A very recent feed-forward competitor, **SHOW** ("Scene and Human in One World: Reconstruction in a Feedforward Pass," arXiv 2606.27720, submitted June 26, 2026) couples scene point-map prediction and SMPL-X decoding bidirectionally in a single forward pass (built on VGGT), with a 3-loss joint fine-tuning stage (scale consistency, temporal stability, SMPL-guided Chamfer alignment). On EMDB-2, SHOW reports W-MPJPE=262.3mm vs JOSH3R's 661.7mm — a **[VERIFIED] ~2.52x improvement**, confirmed via direct table cross-check. Critically, however, JOSH's own (slow, optimization-based) result on the same table is still the best of all methods listed at W-MPJPE=174.7mm — meaning as of mid-2026, iterative contact-aware optimization still beats every feed-forward alternative on accuracy, even though feed-forward methods are closing the gap fast (https://arxiv.org/html/2606.27720v1). SHOW's code is not yet public — its GitHub link is an unpopulated placeholder (https://bowieshi.github.io/SHOW-project-page/) — so it is a pattern to track, not to adopt this cycle. Neither SHOW nor JOSH3R handle ball/paddle objects at all; both are human+scene only.

## 3. Camera+human joint reconstruction lineage — do we even need most of it?

A well-established 2024-2026 lineage (WHAC CVPR2024, GVHMR SIGGRAPH Asia 2024, TRAM ECCV2024, WATCH 2025, SynCHMR 2024, PhysDynPose/MoviCam 2025, OnlineHMR 2026) all target the sub-problem of recovering metric camera trajectory *and* human motion jointly from RGB video alone, because normal monocular SLAM cannot resolve absolute scale without background depth or known-object priors (https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.17346, https://arxiv.org/html/2409.06662v1, https://arxiv.org/html/2507.17406v1). TRAM's masked-DROID-SLAM+ZoeDepth recipe cuts EMDB root trajectory error (RTE) from WHAM's 4.6% to 1.4% and camera ATE from 2.42m to 0.32m — but this entire apparatus solves a problem **we largely don't have**: our ARKit stream already supplies metric per-frame camera pose, intrinsics, and gravity directly from the device. GVHMR's Gravity-View coordinate frame needs exactly these two signals (gravity + camera view direction) and normally estimates them via DPVO — feeding measured ARKit gravity/pose in place of DPVO's own estimates is a concrete, low-risk near-term integration that removes GVHMR's own weakest link (code: github.com/zju3dv/GVHMR, custom **non-commercial** research license, verbatim from LICENSE file).

**PhysDynPose + MoviCam** (CVPRW 2025) is the closest precedent to our worst-performing case (handheld camera + non-flat ground + contact): it replaces the flat-ground assumption with a queried scene height map and adds a future-frame root-supervision term, cutting foot-sliding to 3.22mm on moving-camera non-flat sequences vs 4DHumans' 13.99mm baseline (MIT-licensed code at github.com/aidilayce/PhysDynPose). But even after this fix, scene-penetration rate remains 68.13% of non-flat frames — a reminder that "physically plausible" fixes published so far are partial, not solved (https://arxiv.org/html/2507.17406v1).

## 4. Ball mid-air 3D lifting — filling the stated gap directly

"**Where Is The Ball**" (CVsports @ CVPR 2025, arXiv 2506.05763) is the most directly on-point precedent for our "no mid-air 3D ball, only ground-plane events + single net-plane" gap. It converts each 2D tracked pixel into a camera-independent canonical representation (ray intersected with a ground plane and a vertical plane), then cascades bidirectional LSTMs to predict end-of-trajectory probability, height, and refinement residuals — trained **entirely on synthetic PhysX/Unity trajectories**, no real video in training. On real tennis broadcast footage (tracked via TrackNet), it cuts landing-point error from 3.58m (SynthNet baseline) to 0.63m and F1 from 0.358 to 0.807 (https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05763). This design is compatible with our offline compute and known court/net/ball-diameter priors (we could generate our own synthetic pickleball trajectories rather than waiting for their promised-but-unreleased code/dataset).

**TT4D** (arXiv 2605.01234, May 2026, ACM MM 2026) inverts the standard order for table tennis: it lifts the ENTIRE unsegmented 2D ball track to continuous 3D (with per-frame spin) via a transformer *before* segmenting shots, because 2D-domain segmentation collapses under occlusion — the same failure mode we have during fast net-side occlusion. It then recovers **racket 6DoF pose and velocity at contact purely from the reconstructed ball trajectory**, via an inverse optimal-control problem: single-shooting RK4 integration against the full ball-flight ODE (drag + Magnus), minimizing the L2 distance between predicted and observed post-contact bounce location. This achieves 26.4±4.4° orientation error and 0.58±0.40 m/s velocity error against 92 mocap-verified strokes — **without ever training a racket detector** (https://arxiv.org/html/2605.01234). This is a directly transplantable recipe for our currently render-only paddle problem, at least for the contact instant.

Critically, TT4D itself confirms — even as a dedicated 2026 racket-sport 4D pipeline — that its human-mesh (4DHumans) and ball-trajectory subsystems "**remain independent**," stated as: the 3D trajectory "is reconstructed independently of human-pose tracking," a deliberate choice made so the method generalizes to doubles matches (https://arxiv.org/html/2605.01234). [Note: an earlier draft claim that the paper explicitly says "no explicit collision constraints between them" used a fabricated quote — the word "collision" does not appear in the paper at all; the underlying independence claim is nonetheless accurate per the verbatim sentence above.] This reinforces that joint 3D ball+paddle+human fusion is genuinely unsolved in the literature, not just in our pipeline.

**MonoTrack** (CVPR2022 workshop, arXiv 2204.01899) and **TT3D** (arXiv 2504.10035) offer cheaper stopgap heuristics worth considering before any full joint optimizer exists: MonoTrack anchors 3D shuttle-trajectory endpoints to the hitting player's pose (feet position + assumed ~2m hand/racket height), improving synthetic reconstruction error from 14.9cm to 8.0cm; TT3D disambiguates hit-vs-bounce purely from ball-trajectory geometry (direction change along the table's long axis = bounce, any other kink = strike), but explicitly breaks under moving/handheld cameras — a direct red flag for our handheld-iPhone case (https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.01899, https://arxiv.org/html/2504.10035v2).

## 5. Paddle 2D detection — a reusable, permissively-licensed asset

**RacketVision** (AAAI 2026 Oral, arXiv 2511.17045) is a fresh, large (1,672 clips / 435,179 frames / 942 pro matches, table tennis/tennis/badminton) dataset pairing 2D ball annotations with 5-keypoint articulated racket pose (top/bottom/handle/left/right), achieving racket-pose PCK@0.2 of 81.8–89.6% and MPJPE 5.0–9.71px with an RTMDet-M+RTMPose-M pipeline. Code is **MIT-licensed** with pretrained checkpoints at github.com/OrcustD/RacketVision — a directly reusable, low-cost transfer/pretraining source for our currently render-only paddle detector (https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.17045, https://github.com/OrcustD/RacketVision/). It is important to note this benchmark is confirmed **strictly 2D** — no 3D reconstruction, no racket-ball collision/contact modeling — which itself is further evidence that even the newest, most explicitly human-object-interaction-focused racket-sports benchmark hasn't addressed our joint-3D-fusion goal.

## 6. Known-geometry priors as the metric-scale anchor — a same-genre precedent

**WorldPose** (2025, arXiv 2501.02771, built from 2022 FIFA World Cup footage) is the closest same-genre precedent for using known court/field dimensions to anchor global multi-person 3D consistency: it calibrates both static (16-18 camera) and moving broadcast cameras by fitting reprojected field lines against the KNOWN 105×68m pitch dimensions, then triangulates/bundle-adjusts all players into one consistent global 3D world, validated at G-MPJPE=80.0mm/PA-MPJPE=66.3mm against Vicon ground truth (https://arxiv.org/html/2501.02771v2). Tellingly, on WorldPose itself, standard monocular global-trajectory SOTA methods fail badly *even given ground-truth cameras*: SLAHMR-with-GT-cameras gets G-MPJPE=5,837.2mm and 10.7cm drift per meter traveled, while single-frame (non-global) pose baselines like HybrIK achieve 78.8mm PA-MPJPE — demonstrating that **per-frame pose accuracy is not the bottleneck in full-court multi-person scenes; global trajectory drift is**. This directly validates prioritizing a global-consistency fusion stage over further per-frame pose-model improvements for our own goal.

## 7. Production ceiling context (Hawk-Eye) — use with correction

Hawk-Eye is frequently cited as the "SOTA ball placement" ceiling. Per Wikipedia (the actual source checked), the company's own marketing states accuracy "within 2.6mm" and separately "an average error of 3.6mm" (5% of a 67mm tennis ball), with ITF certification requiring the system stay within 5mm of the true recorded bounce, achieved via 6-10 fixed, pre-calibrated, high-speed cameras triangulating a known static venue geometry (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawk-Eye). **A commonly repeated "2.2mm" figure and "340fps" figure are NOT actually stated on this Wikipedia page** — an adversarial check refuted this attribution — so these numbers should be sourced independently before use. The key structural point stands regardless of the exact mm figure: Hawk-Eye's precision comes from *dense multi-camera triangulation of a known static rig*, a fundamentally different (far more constrained) problem than our single moving iPhone; it should not be treated as a monocular-achievable benchmark.

## 8. A live blind spot: nobody uses LiDAR/depth for this problem

Across all ~20 primary sources reviewed for this report (JOSH, TRAM, GVHMR, WHAM, SHOW, TROPHIES, PhysDynPose, WorldPose, UniCon3R, etc.), **none ingest LiDAR or RGB-D depth** — every method solves the harder RGB-only version of metric scale recovery via priors (height, known court/pitch dimensions, contact constraints) precisely because standard broadcast/YouTube video has no native depth channel. Our iPhone stack, by contrast, provides ARKit metric camera pose, gravity, and LiDAR-Pro depth as first-class per-frame signals. This is either a genuine opportunity (several "hard" sub-problems in this literature, like camera-scale recovery and ground-plane grounding, could become comparatively easy for us) or a sign that no one has needed to solve it this way yet — but no existing recipe was found to adapt directly; this would likely need to be designed from scratch rather than transplanted.

## Constraint callouts

- **Single consumer camera**: nearly all reviewed methods (JOSH, TRAM, GVHMR, PhysDynPose, WorldPose's moving-camera branch) are explicitly built for monocular/single-camera capture, so architecture transplant is realistic; but several (TT3D) assume static/simple-motion cameras and explicitly break under handheld motion.
- **Cloud A100, offline whole-clip compute**: matches every reviewed method's assumption — none are real-time; JOSH's 0.8 FPS full optimizer and TRAM's SLAM+transformer pipeline are both "offline batch" by design, well within our stated compute model.
- **Internal-only licensing acceptable**: still worth tracking exact license text — GVHMR is non-commercial-research-only (a real constraint even for internal use, verbatim from its LICENSE file), JOSH's license is unconfirmed (GitHub API returns null), RacketVision and PhysDynPose are cleanly MIT.
- **Scarce in-domain pickleball data**: every promising ball/paddle-lifting recipe found (Where Is The Ball, TT4D's synthetic generator) is trained substantially or entirely on synthetic physics simulation rather than large labeled real datasets — meaning our lack of in-domain footage is not necessarily blocking, provided we can build an analogous simulator using our known court/net/ball-diameter constants.


## Ranked options

### A. Contact-coupled joint optimization (JOSH-pattern), specialized to our court+player+ball+paddle contacts, initialized from ARKit pose/gravity instead of SLAM
- **what:** Adopt JOSH's architecture directly: keep existing per-subsystem outputs (court homography, camera pose, per-player mesh, ball events, paddle boxes) purely as INITIALIZATION, then add one test-time optimization stage with hinge/consistency losses on physically meaningful contacts — foot-on-court-plane (replacing current foot-pin heuristic), ball-in-paddle-face at contact frames, ball-on-court-plane at bounce frames — using our KNOWN court/net/ball-diameter/player-height priors as hard constraints rather than learned scale factors.
- **evidence:** JOSH's contact-scale loss alone cut SLOPER4D WA-MPJPE100 from 314.3mm to 148.9mm (53% relative drop), and full joint optimization to 120.0mm, beating TRAM/WHAM/SLAHMR on EMDB/SLOPER4D/RICH [VERIFIED core comparison, per-dataset]; PhysDynPose shows the analogous height-map+future-root-supervision fix cuts foot-sliding ~4x on moving-camera non-flat-ground data. https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.02158 , https://arxiv.org/html/2507.17406v1
- **expected_gain:** Directly targets our stated failure modes (floating/penetrating feet, non-provable ball-paddle contact, no global consistency) with a proven-effective coupling mechanism; because we already HAVE metric camera pose+gravity (ARKit), we can likely skip JOSH's slowest/most error-prone component (SLAM+MASt3R scene reconstruction) entirely and only need the contact-optimization layer, which is cheaper than the full published pipeline.
- **cost:** High engineering cost: need to build/train our own contact estimators (DECO/BSTRO-equivalent for feet; nothing published for paddle-ball contact — must design from scratch), plus a differentiable optimizer over multi-hundred-frame windows; JOSH runs 0.8 FPS on RTX4090 even without our added ball/paddle terms, offline-only, consistent with our A100/offline budget but not cheap engineering time.
- **risk:** JOSH's own stated failure mode: 'works best when contact points on human and scene are both visible in 2D' — fast net-side ball occlusion and finger/paddle-grip ambiguity (noted in our racket-6dof lane) may violate this precondition often; also JOSH chunks sequences >=200 frames rather than doing one global optimization, so a naive port won't automatically fix long-clip drift.
- **confidence:** med

### B. Cheap ball-paddle contact prior first (MonoTrack/TT4D-style), as a fast stopgap before any full joint optimizer
- **what:** Before building a general contact-aware optimizer, add TT4D's inverse-OCP recipe (fit RK4 ball-flight ODE between hit and next bounce, solve for racket 6DoF at contact) and/or MonoTrack's cheap hit/receive position prior (anchor ball trajectory endpoints to the paddle-holding hand's known 3D position/height) directly on top of our EXISTING lifted 3D ball events and player mesh, with zero new detector training required.
- **evidence:** TT4D's inverse-OCP achieves 26.4+/-4.4 deg racket orientation error and 0.58+/-0.40 m/s velocity error from ball trajectory alone, no racket detector; MonoTrack's pose-anchoring prior improved synthetic 3D shuttle reconstruction error from 14.9cm to 8.0cm using only feet position + assumed hand height. https://arxiv.org/html/2605.01234 , https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.01899
- **expected_gain:** Low-cost, high-leverage first step toward 'ball provably meets paddle' — gives an approximate paddle pose/velocity at contact for free from data we may already have (lifted ball arc + court/net geometry), without waiting on a full joint-fusion research program.
- **cost:** Low: mostly optimization/scripting against existing outputs, no new model training, runs in seconds per contact event.
- **risk:** Only covers the discrete contact instant, not continuous paddle 6DoF or full-body consistency; TT4D's own residual error is concentrated in Z-velocity/rotation due to unmodeled paddle restitution properties, and this approach does nothing for the feet-floating/mesh-penetration half of the goal.
- **confidence:** high

### C. Ball mid-air 3D lifting via synthetic-trained canonical-plane network (Where-Is-The-Ball pattern), exploiting known court+net geometry
- **what:** Train a camera-independent 2D-to-3D ball lifting network (ground-plane + vertical-plane ray-intersection representation, cascaded BiLSTMs for end-of-trajectory/height/refinement) purely on synthetic physics trajectories generated with OUR known court (20x44ft) and net (36/34in) geometry, to close the 'no mid-air 3D ball' gap that currently only has ground-plane events + a single net-plane.
- **evidence:** On real tennis broadcast footage (different sport, same monocular setting), this design cut landing-point error from 3.58m (SynthNet baseline) to 0.63m and F1 from 0.358 to 0.807, despite training on 100% synthetic PhysX data. https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05763
- **expected_gain:** Fills a named, concrete gap (mid-air 3D ball) with a design proven to transfer sim-to-real in an adjacent sport; synthetic training data generation is essentially free given our known court/net/ball-diameter priors and offline A100 compute.
- **cost:** Medium: need to build a pickleball-specific physics simulator (drag/no-Magnus since pickleball has less spin than tennis topspin, but bounce dynamics differ from tennis clay/hard courts) and re-implement/retrain the 3-network cascade; code/dataset not yet publicly released as of this check, so no head start from a repo.
- **risk:** Assumes first/last frames of each input sequence touch the ground (stated limitation) — may not hold for many pickleball rally segments (net-height dinks, mid-air volleys) without careful segment framing; no code released to bootstrap from.
- **confidence:** med

### D. Feedforward joint scene+human distillation path (JOSH3R/SHOW-style two-tier: slow optimizer bootstraps a fast learned model)
- **what:** Follow the optimize-then-distill recipe: first get option A's slow contact-aware optimizer working correctly on a batch of our own clips (even at ~1 FPS, offline/A100 is fine), then use its outputs as pseudo-labels to train a fast feedforward student network for near-real-time (or at least much cheaper) inference on future clips.
- **evidence:** JOSH3R (distilled from JOSH's optimizer over ~20hrs/3,175 clips of web video) runs at 15.4 FPS vs JOSH's 0.8 FPS (~19x speedup) at a real but bounded accuracy cost; SHOW (June 2026, feedforward-only, BEDLAM2.0-trained) already beats JOSH3R's own accuracy by >2.5x on EMDB-2 W-MPJPE (262.3mm vs 661.7mm) [VERIFIED] while remaining feedforward, suggesting the feedforward-quality frontier is moving fast.
- **expected_gain:** Would eventually decouple per-clip processing cost from the expensive optimization stage, useful if throughput/cost becomes the bottleneck once accuracy is solved; SHOW's numbers suggest feedforward joint human-scene models are a viable, rapidly improving alternative architecture family worth tracking even if not adopted immediately.
- **cost:** Very high: requires first solving option A, then a second training program (data curation, multi-stage training akin to SHOW's 3-stage BEDLAM2.0 pipeline on B200-class GPUs) — clearly a phase-2+ investment, not a near-term deliverable.
- **risk:** JOSH itself (the slow optimizer) still posts the best accuracy of any method surveyed as of mid-2026 (174.7mm on EMDB2 vs SHOW's 262.3mm) — meaning distillation would presently trade away accuracy we may not be able to afford to lose, and neither JOSH3R nor SHOW handle ball/paddle objects at all (both are human+scene only).
- **confidence:** low

### E. Reuse RacketVision (MIT-licensed) as pretraining/transfer source for a 2D paddle-keypoint detector, then lift into our metric 3D court
- **what:** Fine-tune RacketVision's RTMDet-M+RTMPose-M 5-keypoint (top/bottom/handle/left/right) racket-pose pipeline on our own pickleball paddle footage, then lift the resulting 2D keypoints into our metric 3D world using our already-known court homography + camera pose + (eventually) contact constraints from option A/B.
- **evidence:** RacketVision (AAAI 2026 Oral) reports racket-pose PCK@0.2 of 81.8-89.6% and MPJPE 5.0-9.71px across table tennis/tennis/badminton with this exact architecture, and ships pretrained checkpoints + MIT license via github.com/OrcustD/RacketVision.
- **expected_gain:** Cheap head start on 2D paddle keypointing (rather than pure render-only paddle as today) using a permissively-licensed, already-trained, cross-racket-sport model as initialization — directly improves our currently weakest subsystem (paddle is render-only).
- **cost:** Low-medium: fine-tuning existing checkpoints on our footage, no novel architecture needed; still strictly 2D, so a separate 3D-lifting step (options A/B) is required on top.
- **risk:** RacketVision itself is confirmed strictly 2D with zero 3D or contact/collision modeling — it only solves the keypoint-detection half of our paddle problem, not the 6DoF-in-3D or ball-contact half; pickleball paddles differ geometrically (solid short paddle vs strung racket/table-tennis bat) so transfer may be imperfect without our own annotated data.
- **confidence:** med

- RacketVision (AAAI 2026 Oral, arXiv 2511.17045) is a 1,672-clip/435,179-frame dataset across table tennis/tennis/badminton with paired 2D ball + 5-keypoint articulated racket-pose annotations; official code is MIT-licensed at github.com/OrcustD/RacketVision with pretrained checkpoints — a directly reusable pretraining source for our paddle 2D-keypoint detector before lifting into our metric 3D court — https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.17045 , https://github.com/OrcustD/RacketVision/
- RacketVision itself is confirmed strictly 2D (image-plane ball position + 2D racket keypoints, no 3D reconstruction, no explicit racket-ball collision/contact modeling) — reinforcing that joint 3D ball+paddle fusion remains an open, unaddressed problem even in the newest dedicated racket-sport benchmark — https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.17045
- WorldPose (2025, arXiv 2501.02771) builds global 3D player trajectories for an entire 105x68m soccer pitch by exploiting KNOWN pitch dimensions to calibrate both static and moving broadcast cameras (photometric refinement against field-line distance maps), then triangulates+bundle-adjusts multiple players into one consistent world; validated at G-MPJPE 80.0mm / PA-MPJPE 66.3mm against Vicon ground truth — the closest same-genre precedent for using known court dimensions as the anchor for global multi-person 3D consistency — https://arxiv.org/html/2501.02771v2
- On WorldPose, monocular global-trajectory SOTA baselines fail badly even given ground-truth cameras: SLAHMR-with-GT-cameras G-MPJPE 5,837.2mm / drift 10.7cm per meter traveled, vs single-frame (non-global) pose baselines like HybrIK at 78.8mm PA-MPJPE — showing pose accuracy is NOT the bottleneck for full-pitch/full-court sports scenes, GLOBAL TRAJECTORY DRIFT is — https://arxiv.org/html/2501.02771v2
- MonoTrack (CVPR2022 workshop) anchors monocular 3D shuttle-trajectory reconstruction using a cheap hit/receive-position PRIOR derived from player pose (feet position + assumed ~2m hand/paddle height) rather than full joint optimization, improving synthetic reconstruction error from 14.9cm to 8.0cm — a low-cost stopgap pattern usable before any full contact-aware joint optimizer is built — https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.01899
- TT3D (arXiv 2504.10035) uses a pose-independent geometric heuristic (direction-change along the table's long axis = bounce; any other kink = racket strike) to disambiguate hit vs bounce events purely from ball trajectory shape — but explicitly breaks under moving/handheld cameras since it assumes a static, piecewise-polynomial-consistent camera, a direct red flag for our handheld iPhone case — https://arxiv.org/html/2504.10035v2
- Hawk-Eye (broadcast-sports ceiling case): per Wikipedia, the company's OWN marketing states 'accurate to within 2.6mm' and separately 'an average error of 3.6mm' (5% of a 67mm tennis ball); ITF certification requires staying within 5mm of true recorded bounce; this is achieved using 6-10 calibrated, FIXED, high-speed cameras triangulating known static venue geometry — a fundamentally different (far more constrained, multi-camera) problem than our single moving iPhone.
