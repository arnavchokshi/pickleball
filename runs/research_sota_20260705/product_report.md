# SOTA research: Competitive landscape (pb.vision, SwingVision, ...) + automated coaching from 3D reconstructions + cloud video pipeline economics

Generated 2026-07-05 by manager research workflow (28 agents). Adversarial-verification verdicts inline ([VERIFIED] markers + corrections applied).

## Competitive landscape overview

**pb.vision** is the direct incumbent: a single-camera, cloud-processed pickleball analytics product (phone/tripod upload, no proprietary hardware) whose headline 3D capability is explicitly ball-trajectory-only — "3D Trajectory Maps... 3D visualizations of shot trajectories" for drops/drives/lobs (https://pb.vision/). No public page describes a player-body 3D/mesh/skeleton output; their stack is ball tracking + stats/skill ratings + highlight reels, not full-body reconstruction (https://pb.vision/). This is an important correction to the assumption of a "six-dimension skill rating with named CV metrics" — a pb.vision help article itself frames per-dimension named metrics (net height %, kitchen-arrival %, etc.) as **roadmap aspiration, not a shipped feature**, and explicitly states "computer vision is still error-prone and will never be 100% accurate" (https://help.pb.vision/articles/9831649-pickleball-ratings-ultimate-guide). What pb.vision *does* ship today: shot categorization (drop/hybrid/drive/lob), serve/return depth and speed, court-coverage heatmaps, automatic dead-time removal, and "Coach's Insights" — personalized feedback tied to specific video clips (https://www.mattspickleball.com/blog/pb-vision-review-how-ai-is-transforming-pickleball-strategy-performance, https://pb.vision/).

**SwingVision** is the cross-sport (tennis+pickleball) analogue running fully on-device on iPhone/iPad via the Neural Engine (A12 Bionic or later, iOS 18+), in real time, with no internet required for core tracking [VERIFIED via adjacent primary sources — Apple Newsroom co-founder quote and App Store device requirements corroborate this, though the originally-cited swing.vision/home page is a JS SPA that doesn't render the claim directly] (https://www.apple.com/newsroom/2022/05/swupnil-sahai-and-his-co-founder-serve-an-ace-with-ai-powered-swingvision/, https://apps.apple.com/us/app/swingvision-tennis-pickleball/id989461317). Its ball-tracking model is trained on 500M+ shots from 200,000+ players; a forthcoming two-camera line-calling mode targets 99% accuracy on close calls, implying today's single-camera accuracy is lower (exact figure undisclosed) (https://mwm.ai/apps/swingvision-tennis-pickleball/989461317). Coaching output is framed as "personalized coaching after each session" — a post-session narrative layer over automated stats (speed, depth, accuracy, rally length) and highlights, not 3D biomechanical replay (https://apps.apple.com/us/app/swingvision-tennis-pickleball/id989461317).

**PlaySight** takes a fixed-hardware venue path: "Smart Court" multi-camera installations now at thousands of US pickleball clubs, paired with a new "InSight AI" feature (built with Microsoft/Azure OpenAI) that gives up to five strengths/areas-for-improvement within minutes of a match, debuting first in pickleball and explicitly targeted at recreational players (https://www.dupr.com/post/ai-powered-video-pickleball-ratings-dupr-playsight-duprcoach, https://www.novolleys.com/playsight-installs-ai-powered-smart-courts-across-thousands-of-pickleball-clubs-in-the-us/). Its public pickleball page lists "biomechanical analysis" only as a marketing bullet, with no confirmation it's 3D vs. annotated 2D clips (https://playsight.com/our-sports/pickleball/) — treat as unconfirmed, not a proven capability.

**SportAI** (Norway, TIME Best Invention 2025) is explicitly hardware-agnostic — phone video, court-mounted cameras, or broadcast feed — producing swing-curve-vs-pro overlays, ball-strike timing, and rotation/velocity metrics for tennis/padel/pickleball, and recently partnered with Shark to bring this to pickleball and beach tennis (https://tech.eu/2025/04/01/how-sportai-is-changing-the-game-for-coaches-players-and-federations/, https://sportai.com/news/sportai-and-shark-partner-to-bring-ai-insights-to-pickleball-and-beach-tennis). No public evidence of 3D mesh output; language stays at "technique analysis"/"insights" level.

**Zenniz** (Finland, tennis-only, no pickleball roadmap found) sets the gold-standard fixed-hardware accuracy ceiling: ITF Silver Level Certification for indoor electronic line-calling (June 2025) via 4 fixed cameras + 30 court-mounted audio sensors, ~7mm error margin cited on a separate marketing page, ~€10,000 hardware cost per court plus a €10.90–13.90/month subscription, and a $6M Series A (Feb 2026) to expand into a US HQ (https://zenniz.com/smart-corner/zenniz-achieves-the-itf-silver-level-certification-as-the-only-all-in-one-smart-tennis-court-solution-in-the-world, https://www.e-tennis.com/zenniz-lite-outdoor-smart-tennis-court-system-zenniz-lite.html, https://zenniz.com/smart-corner/6-million-fundraise). This category (fixed multi-sensor install) is structurally different from — and unreachable by — a single consumer-camera product; it's useful only as an upper accuracy bound to cite, not a direct competitor.

**IP note**: US Patent 11,615,540 B2 (Maiden AI Inc., filed Apr 2022, granted Mar 2023, active to Apr 2042) broadly claims the *pipeline shape* of single-camera 3D ball tracking — neural 2D detection + camera-projection-matrix calibration + physics-constrained RANSAC/reprojection-error trajectory fitting — not a specific network or solver (https://patents.google.com/patent/US11615540B2/en). Enumerated sports are cricket/baseball/football/soccer/basketball; pickleball/tennis are not named, but claim breadth means a differently-architected pipeline doing the same four steps could still be in scope. This is a genuine legal-review item, not resolvable by further web research.

## Accuracy bar and known failure modes

pb.vision's own "Stat Concerns" help article states **95%+ accuracy on shot detection and scoring** — but only "when our Framing & Court Alignment Guidelines are followed," i.e. under ideal, guideline-compliant conditions, with no independent benchmark or confidence interval given (self-reported) (https://help.pb.vision/articles/6518303-pb-vision-stat-concerns). *[Correction to an earlier draft]*: this 95% figure is **not** stated on the separate "Framing and Court Alignment Guidelines" page, which instead specifies camera height "at least 5 feet" (not "4+ ft") off the ground, full stationarity (tripod/fence mount, no image stabilization/HDR), and all 4 court corners plus all 4 players visible at all times (https://help.pb.vision/articles/1108176-framing-and-court-alignment-guidelines). Documented failure modes from pb.vision itself: low ball-to-court contrast (yellow ball on tan/yellow court), players leaving frame (identity loss), player overlap near the kitchen line and similar-colored apparel (ID swaps), and — notably — camera movement during recording "throws off court alignment," implying no live re-calibration/robustness to motion (https://help.pb.vision/articles/6518303-pb-vision-stat-concerns). Only final match score is currently user-correctable; no per-shot correction tool exists yet.

An independent, detailed user review corroborates real-world failure at the edges: a shot-speed misclassification (60 mph logged for a drive the player says averaged ~27 mph), described as rare; the reviewer calls AI coaching "certainly getting there" but underdeveloped, and requests better unforced-error detail as the top improvement ask; the same reviewer notes that despite many available metrics, they personally use only "4-5 key ones" — **this is one reviewer's account, not a broad user survey**, though it is directly quoted and verifiable (https://www.speakpickleball.com/reviews/pbvision-review).

## Coaching content — what pickleball players/coaches actually care about

Trade-press synthesis (2025) converges on three high-value diagnostic categories that map to the three phases of play: **unforced errors**, **third-shot success rate**, and **dink-rally win rate** — proposed as the minimal stat set worth tracking (https://pickleballdaily.com/quick-pickleball-stats-system-track-3-numbers-to-level-up/). Concretely: ~40% of points in amateur women's matches reportedly come from missed returns/unforced errors ("two out of every five points handed over without a fight") (https://pickleballdaily.com/quick-pickleball-stats-system-track-3-numbers-to-level-up/), and third-shot-drop success benchmarks by skill level run ~50% (3.0 players) → 70-80% (3.5) → 85-90%+ (4.0) under drill conditions, with a 10-20% drop-off expected in live match play (https://www.thedinkpickleball.com/pickleball-third-shot-drop-by-skill-levels-3-0-to-4-0-guide/). Coaches define a "good" third-shot drop not just by landing location but by the *opponent's resulting shot trajectory* (forcing an upward, un-attackable return) — a definition that specifically requires trajectory/physics-level 3D data, not just a 2D landing-spot heatmap (https://centercourtpickleball.com/mastering-the-transition-a-comprehensive-biomechanical-statistical-and-strategic-analysis-of-the-third-shot-drop-and-drive/). Common kitchen-line coaching corrections (stepping back into the transition zone, forward weight-drift/foot-faults, treating the volley zone loosely) are well-documented mistake categories in trade coverage (https://www.thedinkpickleball.com/common-pickleball-mistakes-beginners-make-and-how-to-fix-them-fast/). Notably, pb.vision itself frames injury-prevention and personalized posture/footwork training plans as still "exploring," not shipped (https://help.pb.vision/en/help/articles/9910344-enhancing-safety-and-performance-in-pickleball) — this remains an open, unclaimed feature space among all named competitors.

## Automated technique feedback from 3D/pose data — methods

Two 2025 papers give the clearest architectural precedent for "what you did wrong, tied to a 3D moment": **"Talking Tennis"** (arXiv 2510.03921, Oct 2025) runs a 3-stage pipeline — CNN-LSTM stroke classifier (EfficientNet-B0 + LSTM, 79.17% accuracy on THETIS's 12 stroke classes, vs. 73.96% for prior SOTA SlowFast) → deterministic biomechanical feature extraction (joint angles, limb/racket velocities, stroke-phase timing) → an LLM (GPT-4o, temp 0.2) that is *not* a free generator but is grounded by a rule-based comparator against hand-specified reference ranges, producing a fixed format (score/10 + diagnosis + exactly 3 corrections). Result: 100% format/no-fabricated-number compliance across 317 evaluated outputs, and 3 varsity coaches rated feedback 8.4-8.9/10 on interpretability/actionability for sampled stroke types (n=3 coaches, small sample) (https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.03921). **"SportsGPT"** (arXiv 2512.14121, Dec 2025, athletics/track-and-field domain, not racket sports) provides causal ablation evidence for the same design principle: feeding raw kinematic numbers directly to an LLM craters both Accuracy (3.9→2.85) and Comprehensiveness (3.85→2.4) scores, and removing retrieval-augmented grounding collapses "Feasibility" from 3.9 to 1.65 even when the underlying diagnosis (via its KISMAM module) stays accurate — strong evidence that a rule/retrieval-grounded LLM layer, not free narration, is what makes generated coaching feedback trustworthy and actionable (https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.14121). Neither paper is pickleball-specific, and **neither reports end-user (non-coach) reception data** — this is flagged as an open gap. Adjacent evidence: T3Set (2024-2025) is a multimodal table-tennis dataset built specifically to train LLM-coaching pairs (https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/T3Set...), and a 2025 PLOS ONE paper describes a multimodal-LLM table-tennis coaching system targeted at novices (https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article/file?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0317839&type=printable). In golf, Sportsbox 3D generates a monocular 3D skeleton and lets users directly compare biomechanical measurements (chest rotation, hip sway, spine tilt) against pro references (https://deepswing.io/blog/the-best-golf-swing-analyzer-apps-in-2026?lang=en), and a 2025 MDPI paper argues Euler-angle (not quaternion) joint representations are more human-interpretable for translating pose deltas into coaching language (https://www.mdpi.com/1424-8220/25/22/7073) — directly relevant to how we should represent our own pose/mesh deltas for the LLM-facing layer.

## 3D reconstruction and calibration technical literature (closest analogs to our stack)

For **court auto-calibration**, the SOTA single-image sports-field-registration method is "No Bells, Just Whistles" / PnLCalib (CVPR-W 2024, extended arXiv v4): CNN keypoint/line heatmaps + DLT or points-and-lines optimization, beating prior baselines by wide margins on SoccerNet-Calibration and WorldCup2014 (e.g. Acc@5 80.4 vs. 41.3 for best baseline TVCalib on WC14) — code released under **GPL v2.0** at https://github.com/mguti97/No-Bells-Just-Whistles. Critically, the method is **explicitly single-frame only**: the authors state it retrieves camera parameters "from each individual frame without any prior information," and list cross-frame temporal consistency/camera-motion handling as unsolved future work (https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.08401). This validates that our own per-segment trust-band/fail-closed design for court calibration is not redundant — even 2024/2025 academic SOTA hasn't solved mid-clip camera motion.

For **multi-person mesh reconstruction** (the doubles-pickleball analog), MultiPly (CVPR 2024) is the closest match: reconstructs 2-4 clothed people in 3D mesh (not skeleton) from one monocular handheld video, with explicit depth-order and interpenetration losses for close-contact occlusion, evaluated on Hi4D and a purpose-built 2-4 person handheld-camera dataset (MMM) — near-exact stand-in for doubles. But it is a **per-video test-time optimization method costing ~24 GPU-hours per person** (README states training scales as "24 × number_of_people hours," i.e. ~96 GPU-hours for 4 people) — several orders of magnitude beyond our ~565s/5-min-clip A100 budget; code at https://github.com/eth-ait/MultiPly with **no explicit license file found** (verify before any use) (https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.01595). A 2026 preprint, "TT4D" (table tennis, monocular, 140+ hrs of singles+doubles broadcast video with paired 3D ball + 3D human mesh, CC-BY 4.0 dataset license), signals the exact "ball+mesh from one camera, racket-sport, doubles" problem space is heating up as a research target, though full-text access was limited and its occlusion-robustness claims are for ball-lifting, not confirmed for mesh (arXiv 2605.01234 per search snippet — flagged lower-confidence due to incomplete fetch).

## Cloud pipeline cost norms

pb.vision's own commercial API rates give a direct monetization benchmark: **$8/hour of HD (≤1080p) video** (~$1.60/12-min game) and **$12/hour for 4K** (~$2.40/12-min game, requires explicit enablement); access is gated behind an Annual Premium subscription plus manual API-partner approval — not self-serve (https://help.pb.vision/articles/2793895-pb-vision-api-partner-guide). Consumer-side, pb.vision now runs a **minutes-based subscription** [VERIFIED directly] — Starter $19.99/mo (100 min) or $99.99/yr (1,200 min); Premium $49.99/mo (400 min) or $396/yr (4,800 min); charges round up to the nearest second with a **5-minute minimum floor** per upload; minutes roll over indefinitely while the subscription is active (https://help.pb.vision/articles/9743996-subscriptions-minutes-ambassador-program). *[Correction]*: PB Vision's own help docs state typical processing is **"about 30 minutes"** (not "a few minutes to ~10 minutes" as an earlier draft claimed), with videos flagged for manual review reprocessing "within 24 hours" — the earlier draft's attribution of 24-hour delays to "GCP spot pricing" traces only to an unverified public roadmap post, not to any independent benchmark or engineering postmortem, and could not be corroborated (https://help.pb.vision/articles/1981767-video-upload-troubleshooting). One independent reviewer separately reports ~10-minute turnaround in their actual usage — faster than pb.vision's own stated typical figure, illustrating variance (https://www.speakpickleball.com/reviews/pbvision-review). For broader GPU cost context: on-demand H100 SXM runs roughly $2.00-3.35/hr on competitive clouds (vs. $8/hr for GB200), with spot/preemptible discounts of 60-80% commonly reported, and Snap Inc. reports a $6.2M/yr (78%) cost cut via a spot-instance work-stealing queue across ~1,000 T4 GPUs (https://www.gmicloud.ai/en/blog/gpu-cloud-pricing-comparison-for-ai-inference-2025, https://medium.com/lunit/optimizing-gpu-costs-by-leveraging-spot-instances-189e5dfc17ee).

## Where our constraints bite

Every fixed-hardware/high-accuracy competitor found (Zenniz, PlaySight Smart Court) depends on multi-camera or multi-sensor venue installs — a category we cannot enter with a single consumer camera. The one occlusion-robust multi-person mesh method that maps well to doubles (MultiPly) costs ~24 GPU-hr/person, incompatible with our current ~565s/clip A100 economics, meaning genuine occlusion robustness for 4-player scenes likely requires new R&D, not a drop-in adoption of published work. Scarce in-domain pickleball data is a real bottleneck: no pickleball-specific biomechanical reference-range dataset (needed for the Talking-Tennis/SportsGPT-style grounded-LLM coaching architecture) was found anywhere — it would need to be built from scratch. Internal-only licensing is a live consideration for two items: MultiPly's GitHub repo has no explicit license file, and the No-Bells-Just-Whistles/PnLCalib repo is GPL v2.0 (a copyleft license that has implications if incorporated into shipped code, even internally-adjacent).


## Ranked options (synthesis agent; manager re-derives decisions)

### 1. A. Differentiate on capture flexibility (motion-tolerant capture) as the primary consumer-facing wedge vs. pb.vision
- **what:** Market and validate our court auto-calibration / camera-motion-tolerant pipeline explicitly against pb.vision's documented requirement for a fully stationary camera (tripod/fence mount, 5ft+, all 4 corners) — a constraint pb.vision itself blames for accuracy loss when violated even slightly.
- **evidence:** pb.vision's own guidelines require full stationarity and its own stat-concerns doc names 'camera movement' as an alignment-breaking failure mode (help.pb.vision/articles/1108176-framing-and-court-alignment-guidelines; help.pb.vision/articles/6518303-pb-vision-stat-concerns). SOTA single-image field-calibration research (PnLCalib, arXiv 2404.08401) is itself explicitly single-frame/no motion-tracking, meaning even academic SOTA hasn't solved this — a genuine open technical edge if we've solved it.
- **expected_gain:** high — directly addresses a documented, self-admitted competitor weakness with real usability upside (handheld/less-rigid setup for casual users)
- **cost:** unclear — depends on how far along our own auto-calibration lane already is (noted as 'in flight' in our context); needs a real head-to-head validation before claiming
- **risk:** medium — if our tolerance for motion is only marginally better, the marketing claim could backfire under scrutiny; also needs quantified accuracy-vs-motion curve, which nobody (including us) currently has published
- **confidence:** med

### 2. B. Adopt a 'grounded LLM' 3-stage coaching architecture (pose/feature extraction → deterministic biomechanical comparator → retrieval/rule-grounded LLM) rather than free-form LLM narration over raw 3D data
- **what:** Build the coaching-output layer as: (1) extract quantitative biomechanical/trajectory features from our existing 3D reconstruction, (2) compare against reference ranges/bands via a deterministic rule engine (not the LLM), (3) feed only the comparator's structured diagnosis (not raw numbers) to an LLM constrained to a fixed output format (score + N specific corrections).
- **evidence:** Talking Tennis (arXiv 2510.03921) shows this exact pattern achieves 100% no-hallucination/format compliance across 317 outputs and 8.4-8.9/10 coach ratings on interpretability/actionability. SportsGPT (arXiv 2512.14121) ablations show feeding raw numbers directly to an LLM craters quality (Accuracy 3.9→2.85) and removing retrieval-grounding collapses feasibility (3.9→1.65) even when diagnosis stays accurate — strong causal evidence for the architecture choice, not just correlation.
- **expected_gain:** high — this is the core mechanism for our stated 'what you did wrong' feature and both papers give concrete, replicable evidence it works for adjacent racket sports (tennis) even though neither is pickleball-specific and neither has been evaluated with end-users at scale
- **cost:** medium — requires building a pickleball-specific reference-range/rule library (stroke-type by stroke-type) from scratch; no such library exists publicly for pickleball
- **risk:** medium — both source papers are recent preprints (Oct/Dec 2025) with small human-eval samples (3 coaches, 60 reports) and neither has been tested with real paying end-users, only credentialed coaches
- **confidence:** med

### 3. C. Price via a metered/minutes-based subscription mirroring pb.vision's structure, informed by their $8-12/hr API cost floor
- **what:** Set consumer pricing around pb.vision's demonstrated willingness-to-pay anchors (Starter $19.99/mo for 100 min, Premium $49.99/mo for 400 min, 5-min minimum floor per clip) and use their $8/hr HD API rate as a reference ceiling for what the market will bear for processed-video access.
- **evidence:** [VERIFIED] PB Vision minutes-based pricing confirmed directly from https://help.pb.vision/articles/9743996-subscriptions-minutes-ambassador-program; API rate confirmed from https://help.pb.vision/articles/2793895-pb-vision-api-partner-guide.
- **expected_gain:** medium — de-risks pricing strategy by anchoring to an established, apparently-viable market price point rather than guessing
- **cost:** low — pricing-model design work only, no engineering cost
- **risk:** low-medium — pb.vision's own reviewer calls its tiered/subscription-gated pricing 'a little confusing,' so copying the structure risks importing the same complaint; also our per-clip compute cost (currently ~565s/5-min clip on A100, thus more compute-intensive than a simple ball-tracker) may not support matching pb.vision's per-minute price if our unit economics differ substantially
- **confidence:** med

### 4. D. Target competitive/tournament-oriented players first rather than casual players for initial GTM
- **what:** Focus initial positioning and coaching-feature depth on serious/competitive players (per pb.vision's own reviewer segmentation), rather than casual players where PB Vision itself is seen as 'excessive.'
- **evidence:** An independent detailed reviewer states PB Vision is 'easily the best tool available today' for players chasing higher ratings/tournaments but explicitly 'excessive'/not worth it for casual players (https://www.speakpickleball.com/reviews/pbvision-review). This is one reviewer's account, not a market survey.
- **expected_gain:** medium — could mean less price sensitivity and higher tolerance for setup complexity/turnaround, but market size for 'competitive pickleball players' specifically is not established in any source found
- **cost:** low — mainly a GTM/marketing decision, minimal engineering cost
- **risk:** medium-high — based on a single anecdotal review, not a market study; no data found on total addressable market size for this competitive-only segment
- **confidence:** low

### 5. E. Deprioritize full occlusion-robust neural-optimization multi-person mesh methods (e.g. MultiPly-style) as a near-term technical direction
- **what:** Avoid adopting per-video test-time-optimization mesh methods (24 GPU-hr/person) as they stand today, and instead continue investing in faster feed-forward approaches (consistent with our documented '565s for 5-min clip' budget) for the 4-player doubles occlusion problem.
- **evidence:** MultiPly (arXiv 2406.01595) is the closest academic match to our 4-person doubles mesh problem (explicit occlusion/depth-order handling, evaluated on a 2-4 person handheld-camera dataset) but costs ~24 GPU-hours per person in test-time optimization — several orders of magnitude beyond our current per-clip compute budget.
- **expected_gain:** medium — avoids a costly technical dead-end; redirects R&D effort toward compatible-cost approaches
- **cost:** none directly (a 'don't pursue' recommendation) — opportunity cost is not building the occlusion-robustness features MultiPly demonstrates if no cheaper equivalent is found
- **risk:** low — this is a risk-avoidance recommendation grounded in a concrete, sourced GPU-hour figure from the paper's own README
- **confidence:** high

## Open questions

- What is our pipeline's actual measured tracking/reconstruction accuracy on real in-domain pickleball footage compared to pb.vision's self-reported (unaudited, no third-party benchmark) 95%+ shot-detection figure? - Does our court auto-calibration and camera-motion tolerance actually beat pb.vision's strict 'fully stationary, 5ft+, all 4 corners visible' requirement in practice, or only in aspiration? This is our clearest potential differentiation claim (per pb.vision's own admitted alignment-break-on-movement failure mode) but needs a head-to-head validation, not just an architecture argument.
- Should we pursue patent counsel review of US11615540B2 (Maiden AI) given its broad claim scope over 'neural 2D detection + projection-matrix calibration + physics-constrained trajectory fitting' pipelines, before finalizing our own ball-arc/BVP safeguard pipeline design? Unresolved legal-risk question flagged by research but requires actual counsel, not further web research.
- No human-evaluation/user-study data exists in any of the LLM-coaching papers found (Talking Tennis, SportsGPT) for pickleball specifically, and none establish that end-users (not just credentialed coaches) find grounded LLM biomechanical feedback valuable enough to pay for — this is the single largest unresolved question for our coaching-output roadmap.
- No published research or product was found addressing single-camera 6-DOF paddle/racket pose tracking specifically (beyond our internal work) — is there any adjacent tennis/badminton racket-orientation literature worth mining before our phase-2 paddle estimator work, or are we genuinely first here?
- Is pb.vision's shift from a credits model to a minutes-based subscription (found in this research, dated after October per a low-confidence secondary snippet) fully complete, or does a legacy credits system still coexist for some users/API partners? Relevant if we want to price-match or undercut precisely.
- Given PB Vision's own reviewer explicitly segments the product as valuable mainly for 'competitive/tournament' players and weak for casual players, and its AI-coaching layer is user-flagged as the weakest part of the product today — is that a validated opening for us, or does it reflect a niche too small to build a consumer business around? No market-sizing data was found either way.
- What GPU-hour cost would our own occlusion-heavy doubles-mesh reconstruction actually require at production quality, and how does that compare to MultiPly's ~24 GPU-hr/person figure — is there a faster feed-forward alternative in the literature we haven't found yet, or do we need in-house R&D to get under an economical per-clip budget?
