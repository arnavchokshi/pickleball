# Monocular Ball 2D→3D in Racket Sports: How Competitors Really Get Their Accuracy
*Synthesis for DinkVision — written against the completed multi-agent research pass, 2026-07-19*

## TL;DR

Nobody found a competitor secret. The public record on PB Vision and SwingVision is thin, self-reported, and in SwingVision's own closest patent, explicitly **skips the exact problem DinkVision is stuck on** (bounce/contact segmentation). The academic literature is more useful than the competitor forensics: it converges on a two-part pattern — a **small trained event/hit signal** feeding a **physics-based ballistic fit per segment**, with most of the *measured* accuracy gain coming from **cheap, zero-label domain-constraint post-processing**, not from bigger models or more labels. That is directly actionable for DinkVision tonight, independent of how the 68x-data-reach re-run lands.

---

## 1. Is trained event/contact detection really necessary, or is there an evidenced alternative?

**Necessary in general, with one validated carve-out.** Every academic monocular racket-sport 2D→3D system found in this pass uses *some* trained event/hit signal:

- MonoTrack (badminton) trains **HitNet**, a 2-layer GRU with only ~16K parameters, to classify no-hit / near-player-hit / far-player-hit from a 12-frame window (arxiv.org/pdf/2204.01899).
- "Where Is The Ball" (CVPR2025W) is trained on **end-of-trajectory (EoT) probability labels** to mark segment transitions — despite being described as "synthetic-only," it still needs trained event-like labels, not pure physics (arxiv.org/html/2506.05763v1).
- SynthNet's GRU explicitly segments shots via hit/bounce detection before the physics reconstruction stage (dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3689061.3689073).

**The evidenced alternative — TT3D-style physics-only bounce detection — is real but narrower than it sounds.** TT3D (table tennis) finds the bounce state by minimizing reprojection error of the flight trajectory against the table plane, with **no trained event classifier at all** (arxiv.org/pdf/2504.10035, project page cogsys-tuebingen.github.io/tt3d/). MonoTrack does the analogous trick for the floor via ray–plane intersection. This works because table tennis / badminton bounces occur against a **known, flat, small geometric plane**. Pickleball court bounces have the same property (known court plane) — this is a plausible, testable, zero-label lever for the BOUNCE sub-class specifically.

**It does not cover paddle contacts.** No source in this pass shows a physics-only trick working for racket/paddle contact events, which happen at arbitrary height and position with no fixed geometric constraint. Tellingly, **SwingVision's own core 3D-trajectory patent (US11045705B2, patent-text detail reported by the primary research pass but not independently re-corroborated on adversarial review — treat as directional) explicitly disclaims solving bounce/contact segmentation**, assuming one continuous, uninterrupted flight per solve. Even the closest commercial analog punts on exactly the sub-problem DinkVision is trying to solve.

**Verdict:** trained detection remains the evidenced necessary path for paddle contacts; a physics-only alternative is evidenced but only for court-plane bounces, not contacts. A hybrid (physics for bounces, trained head for contacts) is worth piloting and would cut the labeling burden on the trained side.

## 2. How did commercial products actually obtain their training data?

**PB Vision.** Founder Mike Arney states on a podcast that he personally hand-labeled ball bounding boxes: *"I learned how to draw a box around a ball to say this is a pickleball. But also, you know, had to do that thousands of times."* Prototyping used Google Vertex AI (free credits); Roboflow was used as an educational/visualization tool before final implementation (elderresearch.com podcast, medium confidence). Rally start/end detection is described as a **separate trained "action recognition model,"** later consolidated into one repo alongside ball/player/court models — i.e., PB Vision's system is (at least) four separately-trained models, not one end-to-end net. **No dataset size, broadcast/tournament footage use, synthetic data, or self-training/pseudo-labeling is disclosed anywhere public** — this is a high-confidence null, not a gap in this research pass. PB Vision was founded 2023 by Arney + Ryan Hampton, seed round $1.15M (Feb 2024), 1–10 employees (pickleballrookie.com) — a team size consistent with "founder grinding hand labels" being literally the whole story, though absence of evidence for a bigger data operation is not proof one doesn't exist.

**SwingVision.** Patent filings (US11045705B2, US11615540B2, US11893808B2 — numbers reported by the primary pass, **not independently re-corroborated on adversarial review**, treat as directional) describe a materially different strategy for the 3D-trajectory piece: the **physics solve itself needs no training data**; only two small upstream perception CNNs do — a pose-estimation network (OpenPose-style) to find the shooter's on-ground foot, and a ball-detection network (MobileNetV2+SSDLite). The calibration keypoint CNN needed "approximately 10,000 annotated training images" and the ball detector "thousands" of example images (exact count undisclosed) per patent text. A later, more end-to-end patent (US11893808B2, granted 2024) describes learning 3D properties directly from paired (2D frame, ground-truth 3D) examples, where the **3D ground truth comes from radar, LiDAR, or calibrated multi-camera triangulation captured during prior real events** — an expensive one-time capture-rig bootstrap, not mass hand-labeling. SwingVision was founded 2015, raised a $6M Series A (2023), has >20,000 paying subscribers and >$4M ARR (blog.seedstage.ai/2025/08/19/swingvision/) — a company with more resources than PB Vision, consistent with the capture-rig-bootstrap strategy being plausible for them specifically.

**Academic scale, for comparison:** TrackNetV2's badminton corpus is 77,000 annotated frames / 26 matches (arxiv.org/pdf/2204.01899); the jhong93 tennis dataset underlying DinkVision's own pretrain is ~33.8K labeled events across 1.3M frames (github.com/jhong93/spot); OpenTTGames has 4,271 labeled events across 12 videos (lab.osai.ai); ShuttleSet has 36,492 stroke-level annotations across 3,685 rallies, built with a **purpose-built computer-aided labeling tool specifically to cut annotation cost** (arxiv.org/abs/2306.04948).

**Bottom line, high-confidence null:** no public source for either PB Vision or SwingVision confirms self-training, pseudo-labeling, or licensed broadcast footage for their production ball or event models. The entire public record for both companies is one podcast quote plus patent filings — this pass exhausted the reachable evidence.

## 3. Is ~70 owner-labeled rich rows (→ a few hundred windows) plausibly enough for a first pickleball fine-tune on a 74.5K-event pretrain?

**No clean yes/no exists in the literature — genuinely open, but here is the best available bracketing evidence.**

**Pessimistic bound (from-scratch few-shot):** UMEG-Net formalizes a "k-clip" few-shot protocol (k ∈ {15,25,50,100} annotated *clips*) across five sports (arxiv.org/html/2511.14186v1). At the **largest tested budget of 100 clips**, tennis — the closest racket-sport analog to pickleball — is the **hardest domain**, plateauing at single-digit-to-low-teens F1-Event (two swept reads of this exact paper disagree on the number: 12.5% vs. 9.4%/31.7% F1/Edit — **this numeric discrepancy is itself unresolved and should be flagged, not silently picked**). "From Skeletons to Pixels" studies the same 50–100-clip regime and needs distillation tricks (AWD/AMD-FED) just to beat single-modality baselines under that budget (arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22839). If DinkVision's few-hundred windows were being trained from scratch, this literature says: expect weak absolute accuracy.

**Optimistic counter-evidence (pretrain + small fine-tune, DinkVision's actual plan):** T-DEED shows large-scale broadcast pretraining (500+ matches) followed by dense fine-tuning on a small target set beats from-scratch training with meaningful SOTA gains (arxiv.org/html/2404.05392). A hobbyist project fine-tuned TrackNetV2 — pretrained on 55,563 badminton frames — on manually labeled frames from **a single PPA Tour pickleball match** (exact count undisclosed) and reached 94.07% accuracy / 93.47% TPR / 87.81% precision on pickleball ball **detection**, though the author explicitly flags likely overfitting from single-match diversity and recommends more diverse training video (hudsong.dev/pickleball). This is not an event-classification analog and is an easier task than typed contact detection, but it is direct evidence that "large public pretrain + a small in-domain pickleball fine-tune" is a working pattern in exactly this domain family.

**Net:** DinkVision's plan (74.5K-event pretrain + ~70 rich rows) sits structurally closer to the T-DEED/TrackNetV2 pattern (encouraging) than to UMEG-Net's from-scratch k=100 setting (discouraging) — but no source directly tests "pretrain + k~100-300 windows" for typed event classification. **Only DinkVision's own held-out eval against the protected 50-row seed will answer this.** Plan for a wide outcome range, not a single expected number.

## 4. Which semi-supervised route has the strongest evidence for multiplying ~100 labels without more human labeling — and is it practical?

Ranked by strength of evidence found in this pass:

1. **Physics/domain-constraint post-processing (strongest, zero-label, immediately practical).** MonoTrack applies dynamic programming over rally structure — hits ≤ rally duration, minimum 0.5s spacing between hits, alternating-player-hit constraint — on top of its raw HitNet output. This lifts recall from **78.1% → 94.3%** and F1 to **0.946**, using **zero additional labels** (arxiv.org/pdf/2204.01899). This is the single most quantitatively evidenced lever in the entire research pass and is deployable today on DinkVision's existing event head output, independent of tonight's retrain.
2. **Physics-consistency pseudo-label filtering (good in-domain evidence, moderate effort).** TT4D auto-generates/filters event pseudo-labels across 45,946 broadcast table-tennis games by requiring game-logic consistency (exactly one bounce per segment, identifiable hit points) rather than manual labeling of the whole corpus (arxiv.org/html/2605.01234). This is a real recipe for expanding effective training data from the pretrain + 70 rows without more hand labeling.
3. **Skeleton/pose-conditioned distillation (matches DinkVision's own architecture advantage).** AWD/AMD-FED transfer skeleton-derived knowledge into visual event spotters under few-shot budgets using unlabeled data (arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22839); UMEG-Net fuses human skeleton graphs with ball/racket keypoints for label-efficient spotting (arxiv.org/html/2511.14186v1). No clean "labels multiplied by N" number was found, but this is directly synergistic with DinkVision's wrist-tracking work (see Q5).
4. **Active learning (named as emerging, no multiplier evidence found).** A 2026 survey names active learning and knowledge distillation as the primary low-supervision paradigms in this literature (arxiv.org/html/2505.03991v1), but no source in this pass gives a concrete "before/after" labeling-efficiency number for active learning specifically. It changes *what* to label next (e.g., ranking uncertain windows for the owner's next batch), not whether more labels are needed — worth a lightweight pilot, not a primary lever.
5. **Audio-visual co-training (weakest evidence — do not pursue).** The only competitor evidence found anywhere is SwingVision's patent (US11615540B2, unconfirmed on adversarial review) listing "sound captured by the video input" as **one of several soft plausibility constraints** (alongside pitch-location and speed bounds) in their ballistic fit — no ablation, no accuracy number, no standalone trained audio classifier. This is fully consistent with DinkVision's own kill of audio anchors (onsets ~3.3/s, near-uniform, at/below chance agreement). Treat as a confirmed dead end for both parties, not an unexplored competitor advantage.

## 5. Does person/wrist tracking measurably improve ball-event detection — is DinkVision's people-tracking advantage real?

**Yes, and this is the cleanest piece of quantitative evidence in the whole pass.** MonoTrack's own ablation: HitNet trained on shuttle-position features alone = **70.2% recall / 68.8% accuracy**; HitNet trained on shuttle + court + **player pose** features = **78.1% recall / 76.4% accuracy** — a directly measured +7.9pp recall / +7.6pp accuracy gain from adding pose conditioning to a hit-detection classifier, in a structurally analogous racket-sport architecture (arxiv.org/pdf/2204.01899).

**Important caveat:** MonoTrack only uses pose to help time-localize *which player* hit, not full wrist/swing biomechanics conditioning at DinkVision's fidelity (4 players + 3D wrists tracked). This is supportive, directionally-strong evidence, not a like-for-like proof at DinkVision's own level of sophistication. UMEG-Net's skeleton-graph + object-keypoint fusion architecture (arxiv.org/html/2511.14186v1) and "From Skeletons to Pixels" (arxiv.org/pdf/2604.22839) reinforce that skeleton conditioning is an active, SOTA-adjacent research direction specifically for the low-label regime DinkVision is in — but neither gives a clean effect-size number against a non-skeleton baseline.

**Conclusion:** the advantage is real and evidenced as exploitable. PB Vision, per the competitor-forensics thread, tracks no people at all. Wiring wrist/swing conditioning into the event head (already designed but not yet trained) is a genuine, evidence-backed differentiator — this should move up the priority queue.

## Ranked "what we should actually do" (grounded in OUR CONTEXT)

1. **Ship MonoTrack-style zero-label domain-constraint post-processing now** — DP over rally structure (max hits ≤ rally duration, minimum hit spacing, alternating-player pattern where applicable) on top of whatever the event head outputs tonight. This is the highest-evidence, lowest-cost lever in the entire pass (78.1%→94.3% recall in the source paper) and does not depend on the retrain succeeding.
2. **Finish training wrist/swing conditioning for the event head.** MonoTrack's pose ablation is direct, quantified evidence this class of feature helps hit detection (+7.9pp recall). This is DinkVision's clearest structural advantage over both pb.vision (no people tracking) and MonoTrack (thinner pose usage) — it should not stay in the "designed but not trained" bucket much longer.
3. **Pilot a physics-only bounce detector against the known court plane** (TT3D/MonoTrack pattern: search for the bounce time/position that minimizes reprojection error). This is zero-label and evidenced — but only for bounces, not paddle contacts — so it should be scoped narrowly to offload the bounce sub-class and let the trained head focus its label budget on contacts.
4. **Build a physics-consistency pseudo-label filter** (TT4D-style) to expand effective training data from the pretrain + 70 owner rows without asking the owner to label more.
5. **Do not revive audio anchors.** Both DinkVision's own kill and the one competitor data point (SwingVision's patent treating audio as one weak soft constraint, no numbers) agree: this is a dead end, not an untried competitor edge.
6. **Treat "~70 rows → few hundred windows" as an open empirical question, not a settled risk.** The literature brackets it between discouraging (UMEG-Net from-scratch k=100 tennis result) and encouraging (T-DEED pretrain+fine-tune pattern, TrackNetV2→pickleball transfer case study) — plan the tonight re-run's held-out eval as the actual answer, not the literature.
7. **Stop spending research cycles trying to reverse-engineer PB Vision's or SwingVision's exact recipe.** This pass exhausted the reachable public record. The honest finding is: small hand-labeled sets + tiny classifiers + heavy hand-engineered physics-constraint post-processing, not an undisclosed semi-supervised silver bullet — and even SwingVision's own patents explicitly leave bounce/contact segmentation unsolved. The unlock is empirical iteration on DinkVision's own data, not further competitor forensics.
